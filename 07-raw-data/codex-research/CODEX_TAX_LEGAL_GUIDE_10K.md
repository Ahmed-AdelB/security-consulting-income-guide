# Tax and Legal Structure Guide for a $10K/month Security Consultant

Version: 1.0
Prepared for: Independent security consultant earning ~$10,000/month
Focus: US federal tax basics with state considerations

## Disclaimer
- This guide is educational and not legal or tax advice.
- Tax laws change and vary by state and locality.
- Consult a CPA or attorney before making elections or filing.
- Security consulting has unique liability and compliance risks.

## Audience and Assumptions
- You are a solo consultant or small team.
- Revenue averages $10,000 per month ($120,000/year).
- You work primarily with business clients.
- You may pay subcontractors or hire contractors.
- You are considering `LLC` vs `S-Corp` taxation.

## Quick Summary
- Start with an `LLC` for liability separation and flexibility.
- Consider `S-Corp` election when net profit is consistently high.
- Budget for quarterly estimated taxes and self-employment tax.
- Track deductions carefully to reduce taxable income.
- Use clear contractor agreements to reduce classification risk.
- Plan for international payments with proper tax forms.

## Glossary (Quick Definitions)
- `LLC`: Limited Liability Company, legal entity for liability protection.
- `S-Corp`: Tax election for pass-through entity with payroll rules.
- `SE tax`: Self-employment tax covering Social Security/Medicare.
- `QBI`: Qualified Business Income deduction (Section 199A).
- `EIN`: Employer Identification Number from the IRS.
- `Form 2553`: IRS form to elect S-Corp status.
- `Form 1099-NEC`: Reports nonemployee compensation.
- `W-9`: US tax form for collecting vendor taxpayer info.
- `W-8BEN`: US tax form for foreign individuals.
- `W-8BEN-E`: US tax form for foreign entities.

## Choosing a Legal Entity
- Legal entity choice affects liability, taxes, and admin burden.
- `LLC` is the most common starting point for consultants.
- `S-Corp` election can lower self-employment tax on profits.
- You can elect S-Corp taxation for an existing LLC.
- State fees and franchise taxes can change the math.

## LLC Basics
- The LLC is a state law entity with liability protection.
- Default tax status depends on number of owners.
- Single-member LLC: taxed as a sole proprietorship by default.
- Multi-member LLC: taxed as a partnership by default.
- LLCs can elect `S-Corp` or `C-Corp` taxation.
- LLCs are flexible for adding members later.

## LLC Formation Steps
- Choose a business name and check availability.
- File Articles of Organization with your state.
- Draft an Operating Agreement, even for single-member LLCs.
- Obtain an EIN from the IRS.
- Open a business bank account and separate finances.
- Apply for required licenses or permits.

## LLC Tax Treatment (Default)
- LLC profit is reported on Schedule C (single-member).
- Net income is subject to income tax and self-employment tax.
- You can take the `QBI` deduction if eligible.
- You can deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses.

## LLC Pros
- Simple setup and low annual maintenance in many states.
- Strong liability separation when formalities are maintained.
- Flexible profit distribution if multi-member.
- No payroll requirement for owners.
- Easy to convert to S-Corp later.

## LLC Cons
- All net income is subject to self-employment tax.
- QBI phaseouts can reduce benefits at higher income levels.
- Some states have high LLC fees or franchise taxes.

## S-Corp Basics
- `S-Corp` is a tax election, not a separate entity.
- You can elect S-Corp tax status for an LLC or corporation.
- Profits pass through to owners like a partnership.
- Owners who work in the business must receive payroll.
- Profit beyond salary is distributed without SE tax.

## S-Corp Eligibility Requirements
- Must be a US entity (LLC or corporation).
- Max 100 shareholders.
- Only allowable shareholders (US citizens/resident individuals).
- One class of stock (economic rights equal).

## S-Corp Election Steps
- Form an LLC or corporation under state law.
- Obtain an EIN if you do not already have one.
- File `Form 2553` with the IRS by the deadline.
- Set up payroll (including state payroll accounts).
- File annual payroll tax forms (941, W-2, etc.).

## S-Corp Payroll Basics
- Owner-employees must receive reasonable compensation.
- Salary is subject to income tax and payroll tax.
- Distributions are not subject to payroll tax.
- You must withhold and remit payroll taxes.
- Payroll providers simplify compliance.

## Reasonable Compensation Factors
- Comparable market rates for security consultants.
- Your billable hours and profit margin.
- Your role (technical delivery vs sales/management).
- Client complexity and regulatory requirements.
- Consistency with what you pay other staff.

## S-Corp Pros
- Potential savings on self-employment taxes.
- Clear separation between salary and distributions.
- Credibility with enterprise clients.
- Can support retirement plan contributions via payroll.

## S-Corp Cons
- Payroll setup and ongoing compliance costs.
- Additional tax filings (Form 1120-S, K-1s, payroll forms).
- Risk of IRS scrutiny for low salary.
- State-level S-Corp taxes or fees may apply.

## LLC vs S-Corp Decision Triggers
- Net profit consistently exceeds $60,000 to $80,000.
- You are comfortable running payroll.
- You want to optimize SE tax without aggressive positions.
- You plan to hire employees or regular contractors.
- You expect stable revenue and predictable cash flow.

## Comparison Table (Simplified)
- Formation complexity: LLC low, S-Corp medium.
- Ongoing cost: LLC low, S-Corp medium to high.
- SE tax on profit: LLC yes, S-Corp partial.
- Payroll required: LLC no, S-Corp yes.
- Flexibility for ownership: LLC high, S-Corp medium.
- Investor compatibility: LLC medium, S-Corp low.

## Decision Flow (Plain Language)
- If you are starting out, form an LLC.
- If profit is inconsistent, stay with LLC taxation.
- If profit is steady and high, elect S-Corp.
- If you plan to bring in non-US owners, avoid S-Corp.
- If state fees are high, re-evaluate the savings.

## State and Local Considerations
- Some states tax LLCs with annual franchise fees.
- Some states do not recognize S-Corp elections.
- City-level business taxes can apply to services.
- Local gross receipts taxes can reduce savings.

## Estimated Taxes Overview
- The US tax system is pay-as-you-go.
- Consultants must pay quarterly estimated taxes.
- Estimated taxes cover income tax and SE/payroll taxes.
- Failure to pay can trigger penalties.

## Quarterly Estimated Tax Dates
- Q1 payment: April 15.
- Q2 payment: June 15.
- Q3 payment: September 15.
- Q4 payment: January 15 of the next year.

## Safe Harbor Rules
- Pay 90% of current year tax, or
- Pay 100% of prior year tax (110% for high income).
- Safe harbor helps avoid penalties if income fluctuates.

## Estimated Tax Calculation Steps
- Estimate gross receipts for the year.
- Subtract deductible business expenses.
- Calculate net profit.
- Apply SE tax or payroll tax as appropriate.
- Apply income tax brackets.
- Divide into quarterly payments.

## Home Office Deduction
- Must be used regularly and exclusively for business.
- Two methods: simplified or actual expenses.
- Simplified: $5 per square foot up to 300 sq ft.
- Actual: allocate rent, utilities, and insurance.
- Document square footage and usage.

## Vehicle and Travel Deductions
- Standard mileage or actual expenses method.
- Keep a mileage log with dates and purposes.
- Commuting is not deductible.
- Business travel requires a business purpose.
- Split personal vs business travel costs.

## Equipment and Depreciation
- Equipment may be depreciated over time.
- Section 179 allows immediate expensing.
- Bonus depreciation may be available.
- Keep purchase receipts and asset logs.

## Education and Training
- Continuing education is generally deductible.
- Certifications like OSCP or CISSP may qualify.
- Training must maintain or improve existing skills.
- New trade or business training may be limited.

## Retirement Contributions (Overview)
- Contributions reduce taxable income.

- Plan administration fees may apply.

## Retirement Options for LLC
- SEP IRA: simple setup, high contribution limits.
- Solo 401(k): allows employee + employer contributions.
- Traditional or Roth IRA: lower limits but easy setup.

## Retirement Options for S-Corp

- SEP IRA based on W-2 wages.
- Employer match structure can be designed.

## Health Insurance Deductions
- Self-employed health insurance deduction may apply.
- Must have net profit to claim.
- S-Corp owners >2% have special reporting rules.

## QBI Deduction Notes
- QBI can be up to 20% of qualified income.
- Specified service trade rules can phase out at higher income.
- W-2 wage and basis limitations can apply.
- Work with a CPA to validate eligibility.

## Sales Tax Considerations

## International Payment Considerations
- Foreign clients may require different tax forms.

- US consultants usually pay US tax on global income.

## Collecting Tax Forms from Foreign Clients
- Provide a `W-9` for US-based clients.
- For foreign clients, you might provide a `W-8BEN`.
- If you are a US person, you generally use a `W-9`.
- Some foreign clients require vendor onboarding forms.

## Receiving Payments from Abroad

- Decide if you will invoice in USD or local currency.

## VAT/GST and Foreign Taxes

## Data Privacy and Cross-Border Work

- Ensure contracts address data transfer and security.

## Contractor Agreements Overview
- Written agreements reduce misclassification risk.
- Define scope, payment, IP ownership, and confidentiality.
- Clarify that the contractor controls work methods.
- Avoid treating contractors like employees.

## Core Clauses for Contractor Agreements
- Scope of work and deliverables.
- Payment terms and invoicing schedule.
- Independent contractor status language.
- Confidentiality and non-disclosure obligations.
- IP ownership and work-for-hire provisions.
- Data security and handling requirements.
- Limitation of liability where possible.
- Insurance requirements (E&O or general liability).
- Subcontracting permissions or restrictions.
- Termination rights and notice periods.

## Statement of Work (SOW) Elements
- Project objectives and success criteria.
- Detailed deliverables and milestones.
- Testing methodology and scope boundaries.
- Client responsibilities and access requirements.
- Reporting format and deadlines.

## Payment Terms for Contractors
- Net 15 or net 30 terms are common.
- Define late payment fees if desired.
- Specify reimbursement rules for expenses.
- Require itemized invoices with dates and tasks.

## Contractor Classification Checklist
- Contractor controls schedule and methods.

- Contractor can work for multiple clients.
- Contractor is paid per project or milestone.

## Client Agreements Essentials

## Liability and Risk Management

## Confidentiality and Data Security

## IP Ownership for Security Work

## Non-Solicit and Non-Compete

## Dispute Resolution

## FAQs
- Q: Can I be an LLC and S-Corp at the same time?
- A: Yes, an LLC can elect S-Corp taxation.
- Q: Do I need payroll if I am the only owner?
- A: If S-Corp elected and you work in the business, yes.
- Q: Is all income taxed even if I leave cash in the LLC?
- A: Generally yes, pass-through income is taxed to owners.
- Q: Can I pay contractors internationally without a W-8?
- A: You should collect a W-8 to document foreign status.
