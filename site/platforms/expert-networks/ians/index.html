    IANS Research (Institute for Applied Network Security) Deep Dive Guide¶

    Version: 1.0 Audience: Prospective IANS faculty, CISOs, security leaders, procurement teams, analysts Scope: Platform overview, faculty model, rates, call types, client needs, income modeling, strategies Authoring note: This guide is a structured, neutral synthesis without internal IANS data Disclaimer: Verify all policies, rates, and eligibility directly with IANS Research Compliance note: Educational content only; not legal, tax, or financial advice

    Goals: - Explain how a security-focused expert network typically operates - Provide practical guidance for faculty considering participation - Model income potential using the requested 0–1000/hr rate range - Offer CISO-focused call preparation resources and topic prompts

    1. Platform Overview¶

    1.1 High-level positioning¶

    • IANS Research (Institute for Applied Network Security) is positioned as a security-focused advisory and research organization.
    • The term faculty typically denotes experienced security leaders who advise clients.
    • The platform emphasizes applied, practitioner-level guidance rather than purely academic research.
    • Engagements typically focus on security leadership challenges and decisions.
    • Clients often seek pragmatic trade-off discussions rather than theoretical frameworks.
    • This guide assumes a consulting-style expert network model, not a product-only vendor model.
    • Validate the current service catalog and engagement rules with IANS directly.

    1.2 Typical services and deliverables (illustrative)¶

    • One-to-one advisory calls with CISOs or senior security leaders.
    • Market and vendor evaluation input for security tooling decisions.
    • Incident response debriefs and lessons learned sessions.
    • Program maturity benchmarking and gap analysis discussions.
    • Architecture reviews for cloud, identity, and network controls.
    • Board and executive communication coaching.
    • Security organization design and operating model input.
    • Risk appetite framing and governance model feedback.
    • Budget planning and ROI framing for security investments.
    • Research briefings and panel discussions.
    • Short written summaries or call notes when requested.

    1.3 Value to clients¶

    • Access to seasoned practitioners with current operational experience.
    • Faster validation of strategy before committing resources.
    • Practical guidance on staffing, tooling, and process design.
    • Insight into what peers are prioritizing and why.
    • Early risk detection through independent expert feedback.
    • Support for board-level narratives and strategic planning.
    • Input for vendor selection, RFPs, and contract negotiations.
    • Clarity on trade-offs between cost, risk, and time.
    • Confidence in remediation plans and incident recovery approaches.
    • Exposure to best practices and pitfalls from real-world deployments.

    1.4 Value to faculty members¶

    • Monetize expertise without running a full consulting practice.
    • Influence the security industry through advisory conversations.
    • Expand professional network with senior security peers and investors.
    • Stay current on market trends across industries.
    • Build reputation as a trusted advisor in a niche domain.
    • Maintain flexibility with optional, part-time engagements.
    • Reinforce executive presence through structured advisory work.
    • Gain insight into emerging technologies and operational patterns.
    • Create learning loops that improve their own security program.
    • Earn incremental income while remaining in full-time roles.

    1.5 Typical engagement flow (illustrative)¶

    1. Client submits a request describing the decision or challenge.
    2. IANS matches the request to relevant faculty expertise.
    3. Faculty receives a request with topic, scope, and timeframe.
    4. Faculty accepts or declines based on fit and availability.
    5. Compliance checks confirm conflicts or NDA requirements.
    6. Call is scheduled and prep materials are shared as needed.
    7. Call occurs and the client gathers the needed guidance.
    8. Faculty may receive optional follow-up questions or surveys.
    9. Billing and payment occur per platform policy and cadence.

    1.6 What IANS is not (important boundaries)¶

    • Not a staffing agency for full-time hiring.
    • Not a substitute for legal counsel or regulatory advice.
    • Not a channel for sharing confidential employer information.
    • Not a sales funnel for pushing vendor products.
    • Not a replacement for in-depth consulting engagements.
    • Not a guarantee of deal flow or income.

    1.7 Indicators of strong fit for the platform¶

    • You have CISO, deputy CISO, or security VP experience.
    • You can explain complex security topics in plain language.
    • You have led measurable program outcomes or transformations.
    • You are comfortable advising across industries and sizes.
    • You can discuss strategic trade-offs and constraints candidly.
    • You can maintain strict confidentiality and conflict controls.
    • You enjoy mentoring and guiding other leaders.
    • You can commit to responsive scheduling and professionalism.
    • You have niche expertise valued by enterprise leaders.
    • You can provide neutral, unbiased guidance.

    2. Faculty Membership¶

    2.1 Eligibility and selection criteria (illustrative)¶

    • 10+ years in cybersecurity leadership or equivalent depth.
    • Prior or current CISO, deputy CISO, or security executive role.
    • Demonstrated expertise in one or more specialty domains.
    • Track record of leading security programs and budgets.
    • Experience with regulated industries or complex environments.
    • Ability to communicate clearly with executives and boards.
    • Willingness to comply with NDAs and conflict policies.
    • Professional references or endorsements when requested.
    • Evidence of continuous learning and modern security knowledge.
    • Comfort with advisory calls and structured conversations.
    • Alignment with an ethical, client-first advisory posture.
    • Availability to take periodic calls or panels.

    2.2 Application or nomination pipeline (typical)¶

    1. Submit application or accept nomination from IANS staff.
    2. Provide resume, bio, and areas of expertise.
    3. Screening call to validate scope, fit, and expectations.
    4. Conflict-of-interest and compliance review.
    5. Rate setting discussion and documentation.
    6. Faculty agreement signing and onboarding paperwork.
    7. Profile creation and tagging for matching.
    8. Optional training on call logistics and compliance norms.
    9. Go live in the network and receive requests.

    2.3 Faculty profile elements that increase match rate¶

    • Clear headline that states role, industry, and core strengths.
    • Short bio emphasizing outcomes, scale, and leadership scope.
    • Specific domains such as IAM, cloud security, OT, IR, or GRC.
    • Industry focus such as healthcare, finance, SaaS, or public sector.
    • Tools and platforms you have led at scale.
    • Key metrics achieved such as incident reduction or maturity gains.
    • Budget ranges managed and org sizes supported.
    • Certifications, speaking engagements, and publications.
    • Geographic experience and regulatory exposure.
    • Languages spoken and time zone preferences.
    • Engagement types you accept and preferred call times.
    • Conflict disclosures and limitations.

    2.4 Faculty expectations and operating norms¶

    • Respond quickly to requests, even if declining.
    • Accept only engagements where you are truly qualified.
    • Maintain neutrality and avoid pitching products.
    • Keep all client and employer information confidential.
    • Avoid sharing proprietary or non-public information.
    • Stay on time and professional during calls.
    • Provide concise, actionable guidance and next steps.
    • Request clarification when scope is vague.
    • Log any conflicts or potential bias promptly.
    • Keep profile and availability updated.
    • Follow platform policies on recordings or notes.
    • Provide feedback to IANS on call quality when asked.

    2.5 Time commitment and workload expectations¶

    • Typical call lengths range from 30 to 60 minutes.
    • Some engagements may be longer workshops or panels.
    • Availability can be low volume or high volume depending on demand.
    • Peak demand may align with budget season or incident spikes.
    • Scheduling flexibility increases match rate and income.
    • Expect occasional no-shows or reschedules.
    • Allocate prep time for complex calls.
    • Set realistic limits to avoid burnout.

    2.6 Compensation model notes (illustrative)¶

    • Most expert networks pay per billable hour or per call.
    • Payment cadence is often monthly or quarterly.
    • Some calls have a minimum billable increment.
    • No-show policies vary and should be clarified.
    • Taxes are typically the faculty member responsibility.
    • Currency and payment method depend on your location.
    • Rates may differ by call type or client segment.
    • Travel or on-site work may be treated differently.
    • Confirm whether there are platform fees or markups.

    2.7 Quality signals that lead to more requests¶

    • Strong client feedback or ratings after calls.
    • Consistent, on-time participation.
    • Clear, structured answers with actionable steps.
    • Balanced view of risks and trade-offs.
    • Up-to-date understanding of current security trends.
    • Ability to translate technical details for executives.
    • Responsiveness to scheduling and follow-up questions.
    • Willingness to share frameworks or checklists.

    3. Hourly Rates (0–1000/hr)¶

    3.1 Rate range overview¶

    • The requested range spans from 0 to 1000 USD per hour.
    • Lower rates may be used for mentoring or early-career advisory.
    • Mid-range rates are typical for experienced security leaders.
    • Upper-end rates are reserved for rare, high-impact expertise.
    • Rates should align with your market demand and differentiation.
    • Rates can vary by call type, urgency, and client type.
    • Always confirm IANS policy on rate negotiation and approval.

    3.2 Rate band guidance (illustrative)¶

    Rate band (USD/hr) Typical positioning (illustrative) When to use
    0 Pro bono or community mentoring Intentional goodwill or platform initiatives
    50 Early-career specialist with narrow scope Low-stakes discovery calls
    100 Emerging leader or niche technical expert Short validation calls
    150 Senior manager or architect Tactical guidance and tool feedback

    800 Global CISO with unique scale Strategic transformation guidance
    900 Distinguished expert with scarce skills Critical decisions or crises
    1000 Exceptional expertise with extreme scarcity Executive-level, time-critical calls

    3.3 Rate drivers and modifiers¶

    • Depth of expertise in a niche security domain.
    • Recency of hands-on leadership experience.
    • Scale of environments you have managed.
    • Regulatory complexity and cross-border experience.
    • Past incident leadership and crisis management exposure.
    • Board and executive communication experience.
    • Demand for your specialty in the market.
    • Urgency of the client request.
    • Call length and preparation time required.
    • Reputation, publications, or public speaking presence.

    3.4 Rate setting worksheet (practical prompts)¶

    • What unique outcome can you enable in one call?
    • How scarce is your expertise compared to peers?
    • What is your minimum acceptable rate for your time?
    • Which call types take the most preparation?
    • What is your ideal monthly call volume?
    • How sensitive are you to scheduling conflicts?
    • How would you price urgency or after-hours requests?
    • Are you willing to offer a lower rate for mentoring calls?
    • Do you want a premium for incident-related guidance?
    • What rate keeps you engaged without burnout?

    3.5 Rate negotiation and boundary statements (examples)¶

    • I focus on enterprise IAM programs; my rate reflects that depth.
    • I am available for 60-minute strategic calls at 400 per hour.
    • Short validation calls can be scoped to 30 minutes at 300 per hour.
    • Incident debriefs require prep and are best priced at 500 per hour.
    • I can offer a reduced rate for non-profit or public sector work.
    • I do not accept engagements below 200 per hour due to time limits.
    • I prefer to decline if the request is outside my core expertise.
    • I can adjust rates for multi-call series with clear objectives.

    3.6 Rate by engagement type (illustrative matrix)¶

    Engagement type Typical length Rate sensitivity Notes
    1:1 advisory call 30–60 mins Medium Most common engagement
    Rapid response 30–60 mins Low Urgency can justify premium
    Due diligence 60–90 mins Medium Requires prep and focus
    Vendor selection 45–90 mins Medium Depends on scope and tools
    Incident debrief 60–90 mins Low High value and sensitive
    Workshop or panel 60–120 mins Medium May justify higher rate
    Written advisory 1–3 hours High Extra effort beyond calls

    3.7 Special considerations¶

    • Clarify whether rates are per billed hour or per scheduled hour.
    • Ask about minimum billing increments for partial hours.
    • Confirm whether prep time is billable or included.
    • Determine how no-shows are handled and compensated.
    • Review policies on follow-up emails or extra questions.
    • Consider a higher rate for high-conflict or sensitive topics.
    • Avoid underpricing rare expertise; it lowers perceived value.
    • Avoid overpricing without strong differentiation; it can reduce matches.

    4. Call Types and Engagement Formats¶

    4.1 1:1 Expert Advisory Call¶

    • Typical length: 30–60 minutes.
    • Best for: targeted decision support or validation.
    • Prep: outline 3–5 key questions and provide context.

    4.2 Rapid Response Consult¶

    • Typical length: 30–60 minutes.
    • Best for: urgent incidents, executive updates, or time-critical choices.
    • Prep: focus on risk triage and decision paths.

    4.3 Vendor Due Diligence¶

    • Typical length: 60–90 minutes.
    • Best for: investor or procurement diligence on security vendors.
    • Prep: request the product scope and current customer context.

    4.4 Vendor Selection and RFP Input¶

    • Typical length: 45–90 minutes.
    • Best for: tool comparison, RFP criteria, shortlist refinement.
    • Prep: build a criteria list and scorecard framework.

    4.5 Program Maturity Review¶

    • Typical length: 60 minutes.
    • Best for: validation of security program roadmaps and gaps.
    • Prep: map maturity levels to risk posture and constraints.

    4.6 Incident Response Debrief¶

    • Typical length: 60–90 minutes.
    • Best for: lessons learned, post-incident architecture shifts.
    • Prep: gather details on attack vectors and remediation steps.

    4.7 Board or Executive Coaching¶

    • Typical length: 45–60 minutes.
    • Best for: messaging, risk translation, and executive narratives.
    • Prep: align with board expectations and key KPIs.

    4.8 Budgeting and ROI Planning¶

    • Typical length: 45–60 minutes.
    • Best for: security investment prioritization and trade-offs.
    • Prep: define top initiatives and expected value.

    4.9 Tabletop Exercise Facilitation¶

    • Typical length: 60–120 minutes.
    • Best for: incident readiness and cross-team alignment.
    • Prep: build scenario outline, roles, and decision points.

    4.10 Product Feedback or Roadmap Review¶

    • Typical length: 30–60 minutes.
    • Best for: vendor product strategy and feature validation.
    • Prep: map feedback to buyer needs and risk drivers.

    4.11 Panel Discussion or Research Briefing¶

    • Typical length: 60–90 minutes.
    • Best for: market insights and multi-expert perspectives.
    • Prep: align talking points and audience goals.

    4.12 Survey or Structured Interview¶

    • Typical length: 20–45 minutes.
    • Best for: research studies and benchmarking input.
    • Prep: expect concise, structured responses.

    4.13 Written Advisory Summary¶

    • Typical length: 1–3 hours of effort.
    • Best for: clients needing a written artifact.
    • Prep: clarify scope, format, and deadlines.

    5. CISO-Focused Clients and Use Cases¶

    5.1 Common client archetypes¶

    • Enterprise CISO seeking peer validation or strategy input.
    • Mid-market security leader building a first formal program.
    • Private equity or venture teams evaluating security posture.
    • Board members seeking independent security perspective.
    • Consultancies supporting security transformation projects.
    • Vendors looking for product-market fit insights.
    • Insurance or risk teams evaluating cyber controls.
    • Public sector leaders aligning with compliance frameworks.
    • MSSPs benchmarking service offerings.
    • Startup security leaders preparing for rapid scale.

    5.2 Common CISO questions and focus areas¶

    • How should we prioritize identity, cloud, and endpoint investments?
    • What is a realistic SOC operating model for our size?
    • Which metrics resonate with the board this quarter?
    • How do we balance zero trust with legacy constraints?
    • What is an effective vulnerability management cadence?
    • How should we structure our incident response retainers?
    • Where should we centralize versus decentralize security ownership?
    • What is a reasonable budget percentage for our industry?
    • How do we attract and retain security talent?
    • Which vendors are delivering real value in this segment?
    • What is a pragmatic path to security maturity improvements?
    • How do we scope third-party risk management efficiently?

    6. Annual Income Potential¶

    6.1 Basic formula¶

    • Annual gross income = calls per month × hours per call × rate × 12.
    • Adjust for cancellations, unpaid prep time, and administrative effort.
    • Include only billable hours for conservative estimates.

    6.2 Illustrative income scenarios (not guarantees)¶

    Steady 4 1.0 300 48 14,400
    Established 8 1.0 400 96 38,400
    Busy 12 1.0 500 144 72,000

    Workshop-focused 2 2.0 500 48 24,000

    6.3 Scenario notes and interpretation¶

    • Starter scenarios are common for new faculty members.
    • Steady scenarios assume consistent monthly demand.
    • Established scenarios usually reflect strong profile visibility.
    • Busy scenarios require active scheduling and fast response times.
    • Elite scenarios assume rare expertise and strong demand.
    • Workshop-focused scenarios require additional prep effort.
    • Mixed portfolios balance steady calls with occasional workshops.
    • Actual demand can fluctuate significantly by season.

    6.4 Income levers beyond rate¶

    • Increase your match rate through a clearer profile.
    • Expand availability windows to accept more requests.
    • Build multiple specialty tags to widen search results.
    • Offer structured workshops or panel participation.
    • Improve client feedback scores to boost future matches.
    • Reduce reschedules by offering firm scheduling windows.
    • Propose multi-call series for larger decisions.

    6.5 Non-billable time considerations¶

    • Scheduling and calendar coordination time.
    • Preparation for complex or sensitive calls.
    • Follow-up questions or short clarifications.
    • Compliance checks and conflict reviews.
    • Professional development to stay current.
    • Administrative time for payments and tax records.

    7. Success Strategies for Faculty¶

    7.1 Positioning and narrative¶

    • Define your core security narrative in one sentence.
    • Emphasize outcomes you delivered rather than tasks performed.
    • Highlight scale, complexity, and risk context.
    • Use specific domains to stand out from generalists.
    • Align your profile with the language CISOs use.
    • Avoid buzzwords without explaining practical impact.

    7.2 Availability and responsiveness¶

    • Respond quickly to every request, even if declining.
    • Keep a few recurring weekly slots open for rapid scheduling.
    • Update your calendar promptly when availability changes.
    • Be realistic about how many calls you can handle.
    • Offer multiple time zones if possible.

    7.3 Call preparation¶

    • Ask for the business goal behind the request.
    • Request context on industry, size, and constraints.
    • Prepare 2–3 frameworks or checklists to reference.
    • Identify any conflicts or sensitive boundaries early.
    • Plan your opening to establish scope and intent.

    7.4 During-call execution¶

    • Start with a short summary of your understanding.
    • Confirm the most important decision the client needs.
    • Use concise, structured answers with clear trade-offs.
    • Share examples and pitfalls without revealing confidential data.
    • Offer next steps or decision criteria before closing.

    7.5 Post-call follow-up¶

    • Provide a brief recap if the platform supports it.
    • Note any open questions for the client to explore.
    • Log lessons learned to refine future calls.
    • Update your profile based on repeated questions.

    7.6 Building long-term demand¶

    • Ask IANS for feedback to improve match quality.
    • Join panels or research efforts to increase visibility.
    • Publish thought leadership that aligns with your niche.
    • Maintain current knowledge of vendor landscapes.
    • Track patterns in requests to anticipate demand.

    7.7 Boundary management¶

    • Decline calls outside your expertise without apology.
    • Avoid sharing sensitive or employer-confidential data.
    • Keep vendor opinions balanced and evidence-driven.
    • Separate advisory work from any sales activities.
    • Pause when requests feel like conflicts of interest.

    7.8 Personal operating system¶

    • Keep templates for common call types.
    • Maintain a private notes system for non-confidential insights.
    • Track your own conversion rate and income trends.
    • Set monthly goals for volume and earnings.

    8.1 Comparison caveats¶

    • This section uses generalized industry patterns, not official claims.
    • Confirm specific policies, rates, and scope with each network.
    • Security-focused networks can vary widely in quality and demand.

    8.2 Comparison matrix (generalized)¶

    Dimension IANS Research (security-focused) Generalist networks Boutique security networks

    Typical clients CISOs, boards, security teams Investors, consultants, corporates Investors, vendors, security teams
    Matching depth Higher depth for security topics Broad but less specialized Deep but smaller pool
    Brand perception Security practitioner emphasis Large-scale expert sourcing Specialist or regional focus
    Engagement types Calls, panels, research, coaching Primarily calls and diligence Calls, panels, research

    8.3 Where IANS Research is often a strong fit¶

    • Security-only questions needing deep practitioner context.
    • CISO-to-CISO strategy validation.
    • Complex security program design decisions.
    • Board and executive communication coaching.
    • Incident response leadership insights.
    • Security talent and org design discussions.

    8.4 Where generalist networks may be competitive¶

    • Multi-industry research beyond security scope.
    • Large, rapid sourcing across many topics.
    • Broader market research spanning finance and operations.
    • Non-security domains where generalists have more depth.

    8.5 Multi-network strategy for faculty¶

    • Maintain consistent positioning across networks.
    • Use IANS for deep security topics and niche expertise.
    • Use generalist networks for broader, cross-domain requests.
    • Avoid conflicts by tracking client categories carefully.
    • Keep rate bands consistent with your market value.

    9. Real Faculty Member Earnings (What Is and Is Not Public)¶

    9.1 What is known from this repository¶

    • This repository does not include any verified earnings data.
    • No faculty names or payment records are provided here.
    • Earnings data for expert networks is typically confidential.

    9.2 Why public earnings data is limited¶

    • Faculty agreements often include confidentiality clauses.
    • Client contracts may restrict disclosure of engagement details.
    • Earnings are highly variable by niche, availability, and demand.
    • Public claims can be anecdotal and not independently verified.

    9.3 How to obtain real, verified earnings data¶

    • Request anonymized aggregate statistics from IANS.
    • Ask for average call volume ranges by faculty tier.
    • Conduct a private survey among trusted faculty peers.
    • Review any official IANS marketing or reports for ranges.
    • Track your own earnings and compare after 6–12 months.

    9.4 Earnings estimation method you can use¶

    • Track number of requests received per month.
    • Track acceptance rate and actual call completion rate.
    • Multiply completed calls by billed hours and rate.
    • Subtract time for prep, scheduling, and cancellations.
    • Adjust estimates quarterly to reflect real demand.

    10. Onboarding Checklist¶

    • Confirm eligibility and scope of expertise.
    • Prepare a concise executive bio and headline.
    • Document key outcomes and metrics from past roles.
    • List industries and regulatory frameworks you know well.
    • Define your preferred call types and lengths.
    • Set a clear rate band and minimum acceptable rate.
    • Review conflict-of-interest policies carefully.
    • Clarify your availability windows and time zone.
    • Provide references or validation if requested.
    • Review payment cadence and invoicing process.
    • Learn platform tools for scheduling and communication.
    • Complete any required training or compliance modules.
    • Test your audio and video setup for calls.
    • Keep a secure system for non-confidential notes.
    • Plan a personal onboarding goal for the first 90 days.

    11. Ethics, Compliance, and Risk Management¶

    • Never disclose employer confidential information.
    • Avoid sharing non-public security incidents or breaches.
    • Do not provide legal, regulatory, or investment advice.
    • Decline calls that present clear conflicts of interest.
    • Keep vendor feedback balanced and evidence-based.
    • Avoid endorsing products you are paid to sell.
    • Respect NDAs and client confidentiality at all times.
    • Ask for scope clarification when the request is vague.
    • Avoid discussing future product roadmaps from employers.
    • Keep notes free of sensitive or identifying client data.
    • Inform IANS promptly if you discover a conflict mid-call.
    • Use secure communication channels approved by the platform.
    • Stay current on ethics guidance relevant to your role.
    • Be explicit about what you can and cannot share.

    12. Frequently Asked Questions¶

    • Q: How often will I get requests?
    • A: Demand varies by niche, profile quality, and availability.
    • Q: Can I set different rates for different call types?
    • A: Many networks allow this, but confirm with IANS policy.
    • Q: What if a request overlaps with my employer?
    • A: Disclose the conflict and decline if necessary.
    • Q: Are prep hours billable?
    • A: Usually not, unless explicitly approved or scoped.
    • Q: Can I do calls outside standard business hours?
    • A: Yes, if you choose, but set boundaries in advance.
    • Q: How quickly should I respond to requests?
    • A: Faster responses typically increase match rate.
    • Q: Can I participate in panels or research reports?
    • A: Often yes; confirm the scope and compensation.
    • Q: Do I need insurance or liability coverage?
    • A: Some faculty maintain coverage, but confirm requirements.
    • Q: Can I bring in co-faculty for a call?
    • A: Only if the platform explicitly approves it.
    • Q: How do I improve my match rate?
    • A: Refine your profile, highlight outcomes, and stay responsive.
    • Q: What if a client asks for confidential info?
    • A: Decline and steer back to general, non-confidential guidance.
    • Q: Is there a minimum commitment?
    • A: Typically no, but expectations vary by platform.
    • Q: How are cancellations handled?
    • A: Policies differ, so confirm no-show rules.
    • Q: Can I publish insights from calls?
    • A: Not without permission and strict anonymization.
    • Q: Are rates negotiable over time?
    • A: Many faculty adjust rates as demand increases.

    13. Glossary¶

    • Advisory call: A short, focused consultation with a client.
    • Call match: The process of aligning requests to faculty expertise.
    • Compliance screen: Review for conflicts or NDA constraints.
    • Due diligence: Investigation of a vendor or target company.
    • Engagement: Any paid interaction between faculty and clients.
    • Faculty: Senior practitioners available for advisory work.
    • Match rate: Percentage of requests you accept and complete.
    • NDA: Non-disclosure agreement governing confidentiality.
    • Panel: A multi-expert session with coordinated discussion.
    • RFP: Request for proposal used in vendor selection.
    • Retainer: Ongoing advisory relationship, often not typical here.
    • SME: Subject matter expert.
    • Tabletop exercise: Simulated incident response scenario.
    • TPRM: Third-party risk management.
    • Zero trust: Security model minimizing implicit trust.

    Appendix A. Call Preparation Checklist¶

    • Confirm the business decision behind the request.
    • Clarify the industry, size, and risk posture.
    • Ask which stakeholders will join the call.
    • Identify any conflicts or sensitive restrictions.
    • Prepare 2–3 frameworks or checklists.
    • Note relevant benchmarks or maturity models.
    • Outline likely trade-offs and decision criteria.
    • Plan a 60-second opening summary.
    • Keep examples generalized and non-confidential.
    • Prepare 1–2 clarifying questions.
    • Decide on key takeaways to share before closing.
    • Keep a timer to respect the schedule.
    • Summarize action items at the end.
    • Log high-level insights for future calls.
    • Update your profile if new topics repeat.
