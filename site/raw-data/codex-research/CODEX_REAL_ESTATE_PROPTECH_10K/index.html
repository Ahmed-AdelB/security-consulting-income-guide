    • Physical security consulting rates and projects remain strong in 2025, with meaningful portfolio demand.
    • OT/ICS security practices translate directly into smart building and property tech environments.

    • Entry points are typically asset discovery, risk assessments, and segmentation planning.
    • The highest-value engagements translate technical risk into operational impact for owners and operators.
    • Scarce OT/ICS expertise drives premium rates and higher project values.
    • Boutique positioning outperforms generic freelancing in property portfolio markets.
    • Smart building security programs blend physical access control with OT segmentation and monitoring.
    • Long lifecycle modernization programs create multi-year revenue streams.
    • Multi-site portfolios enable high-value multi-phase programs and standardized reporting.
    • Cyber-physical consultants command premiums for bridging physical and digital controls.
    • Compliance frameworks (IEC 62443, NIST 800-82) map well to building automation risk profiles.
    • Physical security assessments scale by facility size and complexity.
    • Access control consulting generates both project fees and recurring SaaS revenue.
    • CPTED consulting and property management partnerships deliver repeatable revenue.
    • Data center and healthcare facilities demand high-assurance, converged security programs.
    • Smart building remote access governance is a critical control domain.
    • OT SOC integration and incident readiness are differentiated offerings.
    • Portfolio owners value standardized risk scoring and remediation roadmaps.

    • The cyber-physical market is accelerating as smart assets proliferate across properties.
    • Operational uptime and safety drive non-discretionary security spend.
    • Real estate operators are converging IT and building systems into shared networks.
    • Ransomware increasingly targets industrial and operational environments.
    • Legacy protocol exposure persists in OT networks and property operations.
    • Vendor and contractor access is a recurring risk and control priority.
    • Scarce OT expertise raises rates and elevates project values.
    • Regulation and insurance requirements push formalized risk programs.
    • Multi-year modernization programs create sustained consulting demand.
    • Asset visibility and inventory are required before segmentation or monitoring.
    • Network segmentation is a key step in limiting blast radius.
    • Secure remote access is a common focus in property tech engagements.
    • OT SOC integration reflects a shift toward continuous monitoring.
    • Incident response readiness is mandatory for safety-critical property operations.
    • Safety and availability dominate priorities in OT-informed property security.
    • Property portfolios need consistent risk scoring and centralized evidence.
    • Boutique firms often capture higher rates than freelance generalists.
    • Data centers and healthcare facilities command premium control requirements.
    • CPTED and physical access control remain core services in real estate security.
    • Cyber-physical consultants can command the highest premium positioning.

    4. Real Estate & PropTech Security Scope¶

    • Scope includes physical security, access control, and cyber-physical operational systems.
    • The focus is on property owners, operators, and portfolio managers.
    • Security programs span assessments, design, implementation oversight, and monitoring.
    • Smart building security aligns OT controls with physical controls.
    • Asset visibility and risk mapping are the default entry engagements.
    • Portfolio programs require standardized reporting and cross-site baselines.

    4.1 Asset & Facility Types¶

    • Small facilities (office, retail) used in physical assessment pricing models.
    • Medium facilities (warehouse, school) with higher complexity.
    • Large or complex facilities (hospital, campus, critical infrastructure).
    • Multi-site portfolios that enable volume pricing and programmatic assessments.
    • Multi-family residential properties with CPTED certification potential.
    • Data centers requiring converged physical and cyber controls.
    • Regulated facilities where compliance and safety requirements elevate scope.
    • Facilities with significant contractor or vendor access needs.

    4.2 Stakeholder Map¶

    • Property owners focused on portfolio risk and compliance exposure.
    • Facility managers responsible for access control and operational uptime.
    • IT and OT engineers managing building systems and integration.
    • Security leadership driving enterprise risk and governance.
    • Regulators and insurers enforcing standards and requiring evidence.
    • Vendors and integrators installing access control and monitoring systems.

    4.3 Program Objectives¶

    • Reduce crime and safety risk via CPTED and physical security upgrades.
    • Improve cyber-physical resilience through segmentation and monitoring.
    • Prevent ransomware from crossing IT/OT boundaries.
    • Establish consistent access control policies and visitor management.
    • Build incident readiness that includes operational leadership.
    • Produce audit-ready evidence and standardized reporting.

    5. Threat Landscape & Risk Drivers¶

    • Ransomware causes operational impact when IT compromise cascades into OT.
    • Credential misuse appears in shared engineering or vendor accounts.
    • Legacy protocol exploitation targets Modbus, DNP3, PROFINET, Ethernet/IP.
    • Supply chain and contractor access introduce uncontrolled remote paths.
    • Misconfiguration and unpatched systems are common in OT environments.
    • Safety events and environmental harm are operational consequences.
    • Downtime and production loss translate to revenue impact.
    • Regulatory enforcement creates exposure for weak controls.
    • Brand and supply chain disruption amplify reputational damage.
    • Asset visibility gaps limit the ability to prioritize remediation.
    • Flat networks allow lateral movement across operational zones.
    • Poor remote access governance undermines segmentation benefits.
    • Inadequate incident response plans delay recovery.
    • Lack of monitoring prevents detection of operational anomalies.
    • Weak physical access control undermines cyber protections.
    • Visitor management gaps introduce insider and tailgating risks.
    • Inconsistent evidence collection erodes audit readiness.

    6. Smart Building Security Architecture¶

    • Smart building security inherits OT/ICS constraints: safety and availability first.
    • Controls must protect operations without disrupting process control.
    • Architecture aligns physical access systems with network security policy.
    • Asset discovery is the foundation for any control rollout.
    • Segmentation limits blast radius across building zones.
    • Remote access controls are prioritized for vendors and contractors.
    • Monitoring integrates OT alerts with SOC workflows.
    • Incident readiness includes tabletop exercises with operations leadership.

    6.1 Physical Controls (GRC-aligned)¶

    • Physical access control and visitor management are core controls.
    • Secure areas protect sensitive equipment and critical operations.
    • Environmental safeguards reduce operational risk exposure.
    • Power redundancy aligns with uptime requirements.
    • Equipment protection supports resilience and safety outcomes.
    • Physical controls complement cyber segmentation strategies.

    6.2 OT/ICS-Informed Controls¶

    • Asset inventory with device list, model/firmware, protocol, and criticality.
    • Network diagrams mapping OT zones, conduits, and trust boundaries.
    • Risk register prioritizing operational impact.
    • Segmentation blueprint defining target architecture and phased rollout.
    • Detection strategy mapping OT alerts to playbooks.
    • Vendor remote access strategy and SOPs.
    • Change management and patching roadmaps for OT assets.
    • OT security policy set (access, logging, backups).

    6.3 Secure Remote Access Patterns¶

    • Secure remote access architecture and onboarding is a common service.
    • Access control strategy for vendors and contractors is required.
    • MFA and privileged access controls reduce credential misuse.
    • Remote access constraints protect uptime and safety-critical assets.
    • Access reviews ensure vendor privileges stay current.

    6.4 Monitoring & Response Integration¶

    • OT-specific alert tuning reduces noise and improves triage.
    • SIEM/SOAR integration aligns OT data with enterprise monitoring.
    • Runbooks document OT response procedures and escalation paths.
    • Tabletop exercises validate response workflows.
    • Crash cards and out-of-band communications improve resilience.

    • Large/complex (hospital/campus/critical): $20,000 - $50,000+

    9.3 Square Footage Pricing Model¶

    • Rate: $0.35 - $1.25 per sq. ft.
    • Example: 100,000 sq. ft. assessment = $35,000 - $125,000

    9.4 Access Control System Consulting¶

    • Per-door installation reference: $2,500 - $4,300 per door
    • Consulting fee: 10% - 20% of total hardware/installation cost
    • Example: 50-door project ($150k) yields $15k - $30k consulting fee
    • SaaS access control: $3.50 - $15.00 per door/month recurring revenue

    9.5 CPTED Consulting Rates¶

    • Standard assessment: $2,500 - $10,000 per property
    • Hourly rate: $150 - $300
    • Retainers: ~$2,500 (8-10 hour blocks)
    • Multi-family initial certification: ~$15 per unit (min $400)
    • Multi-family annual renewal: ~$30 per unit (min $600)
    • Multi-family re-assessment: ~$10 per unit

    16. Checklists & Field Guides¶

    16.1 Pre-Engagement Scoping Checklist¶

    • Identify facility type and size category.
    • Confirm number of sites and portfolio scope.
    • Gather existing network diagrams and asset inventories.
    • Identify operational constraints and maintenance windows.
    • Confirm stakeholder contacts across facilities and security.
    • Define compliance requirements or audit deadlines.
    • Map vendor and contractor access paths.
    • Determine required onsite access and safety procedures.
    • Establish data collection and evidence handling protocols.
    • Confirm reporting format and executive summary needs.

    16.2 Physical Security Assessment Checklist¶

    • Conduct facility walkthrough and perimeter review.
    • Validate access control coverage by entry point.
    • Review visitor management procedures.
    • Inspect secure areas and equipment protection.
    • Evaluate environmental safeguards and power redundancy.
    • Capture photo evidence for TVRA reporting.
    • Review policies for access control and incident response.
    • Interview staff on security procedures.
    • Document gaps in secure area segmentation.
    • Review physical control alignment with cyber policies.
    • Identify CPTED improvement opportunities.
    • Assess lighting, visibility, and access control placement.
    • Check alarm response workflows and escalation paths.
    • Evaluate on-site guard coverage and effectiveness.
    • Validate emergency communication procedures.
    • Record facility-specific risks for risk register.

    16.3 CPTED Assessment Checklist¶

    • Review property layout for natural surveillance.
    • Evaluate access control points for territorial reinforcement.
    • Assess maintenance and environmental design for visibility.
    • Identify opportunities for activity support and safe zones.
    • Document property management responsibilities.
    • Align recommendations with CPTED certification criteria.

    16.4 Asset Discovery & Baseline Checklist¶

    • Deploy passive monitoring or data collection methods.
    • Identify asset inventory completeness gaps.
    • Capture device models, firmware, protocols, and criticality.
    • Document OT zones, conduits, and trust boundaries.
    • Validate network diagrams with engineering teams.
    • Map IT/OT interconnects and potential lateral paths.
    • Identify shared accounts or credential misuse risks.
    • Catalog vendor remote access tools and paths.
    • Create initial risk register with operational impact.
    • Define baseline KPIs for future comparisons.

    16.5 Risk & Compliance Checklist¶

    • Map controls to IEC 62443 requirements.
    • Map controls to NIST CSF/800-82 where applicable.
    • Identify gaps in segmentation and remote access controls.
    • Validate backup and recovery procedures for OT systems.
    • Document patching cadence and constraints.
    • Confirm incident response roles and ownership.
    • Validate evidence collection for audit readiness.
    • Assess vendor risk and contract security clauses.
    • Document security awareness and training activities.

    16.6 Segmentation & Architecture Checklist¶

    • Align architecture to Purdue model zones.
    • Define zone and conduit boundaries.
    • Draft firewall rule sets and policy definitions.
    • Create vendor access segmentation plan.
    • Define MFA and privileged access requirements.
    • Map data flows across zones for monitoring alignment.
    • Plan phased rollout to limit operational disruption.
    • Document exceptions and compensating controls.

    16.7 Monitoring & OT SOC Checklist¶

    • Identify OT-specific alert requirements.
    • Tune alert thresholds to minimize false positives.
    • Map OT alerts to SOC triage workflows.
    • Build escalation matrices with operations leadership.
    • Integrate OT telemetry into SIEM/SOAR.
    • Create OT-specific runbooks and response paths.
    • Validate monitoring coverage across critical assets.

    16.8 Incident Response Readiness Checklist¶

    • Build OT-specific incident response plan.
    • Create ransomware response playbook for OT.
    • Define out-of-band communication channels.
    • Prepare crash cards for critical operations.
    • Schedule tabletop exercises with operations.
    • Document lessons learned and remediation steps.

    16.9 Access Control Consulting Checklist¶

    • Define door coverage and access control requirements.
    • Assess per-door installation cost assumptions.
    • Draft system specifications and RFP documents.
    • Evaluate vendors and integrator proposals.
    • Validate SaaS access control pricing and terms.
    • Define commissioning and acceptance testing steps.

    17.1 Baseline Assessment SOW Outline¶

    • Scope: asset discovery, network mapping, and risk register.
    • Method: passive monitoring, interviews, and documentation review.
    • Deliverables: asset inventory, network diagrams, prioritized risks.
    • Timeline: onsite data collection + remote analysis.
    • Pricing: fixed fee based on facility size category.

    17.2 Segmentation & Architecture SOW Outline¶

    • Scope: zone/conduit design and policy definition.
    • Method: architecture workshops and data flow analysis.
    • Deliverables: segmentation blueprint and phased rollout plan.
    • Timeline: phased design sprints.
    • Pricing: time and materials or phased fixed fee.

    17.3 Monitoring Integration SOW Outline¶

    • Scope: OT alert tuning and SOC integration.
    • Method: platform configuration, alert tuning, runbook development.
    • Deliverables: tuned alert set, runbooks, escalation matrix.
    • Timeline: iterative tuning cycles.
    • Pricing: fixed fee plus recurring retainer.

    17.4 Incident Readiness SOW Outline¶

    • Scope: OT-specific IR plan and tabletop exercises.
    • Method: plan development, exercises, remediation planning.
    • Deliverables: IR plan, exercise report, remediation roadmap.
    • Timeline: 4-8 week delivery.
    • Pricing: fixed fee per exercise.

    17.5 Physical Security Assessment SOW Outline¶

    • Scope: facility walkthrough, CPTED review, access control assessment.
    • Method: onsite inspection, staff interviews, policy review.
    • Deliverables: TVRA report, photo evidence, recommendations.
    • Timeline: 2-6 week delivery depending on site size.
    • Pricing: fixed fee aligned to facility category.
