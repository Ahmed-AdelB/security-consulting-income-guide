    Tax and Legal Structure Guide for a $10K/month Security Consultant¶

    Version: 1.0 Prepared for: Independent security consultant earning ~$10,000/month Focus: US federal tax basics with state considerations

    Disclaimer¶

    • This guide is educational and not legal or tax advice.
    • Tax laws change and vary by state and locality.
    • Consult a CPA or attorney before making elections or filing.
    • Security consulting has unique liability and compliance risks.

    Audience and Assumptions¶

    • You are a solo consultant or small team.
    • Revenue averages $10,000 per month ($120,000/year).
    • You work primarily with business clients.
    • You may pay subcontractors or hire contractors.
    • You are considering LLC vs S-Corp taxation.

    Quick Summary¶

    • Start with an LLC for liability separation and flexibility.
    • Consider S-Corp election when net profit is consistently high.
    • Budget for quarterly estimated taxes and self-employment tax.
    • Track deductions carefully to reduce taxable income.
    • Use clear contractor agreements to reduce classification risk.
    • Plan for international payments with proper tax forms.

    Glossary (Quick Definitions)¶

    • LLC: Limited Liability Company, legal entity for liability protection.
    • S-Corp: Tax election for pass-through entity with payroll rules.
    • SE tax: Self-employment tax covering Social Security/Medicare.
    • QBI: Qualified Business Income deduction (Section 199A).
    • EIN: Employer Identification Number from the IRS.
    • Form 2553: IRS form to elect S-Corp status.
    • Form 1099-NEC: Reports nonemployee compensation.
    • W-9: US tax form for collecting vendor taxpayer info.
    • W-8BEN: US tax form for foreign individuals.
    • W-8BEN-E: US tax form for foreign entities.

    Income Profile for a $10K/month Consultant¶

    • Annual gross receipts: ~$120,000.
    • Typical project mix: audits, pentests, advisory retainers.
    • Billing model: hourly, fixed-fee, or retainer.
    • Expenses vary based on tools, travel, and subcontracting.
    • Net profit often ranges from 50% to 80% of gross.

    Choosing a Legal Entity¶

    • Legal entity choice affects liability, taxes, and admin burden.
    • LLC is the most common starting point for consultants.
    • S-Corp election can lower self-employment tax on profits.
    • You can elect S-Corp taxation for an existing LLC.
    • State fees and franchise taxes can change the math.

    LLC Basics¶

    • The LLC is a state law entity with liability protection.
    • Default tax status depends on number of owners.
    • Single-member LLC: taxed as a sole proprietorship by default.
    • Multi-member LLC: taxed as a partnership by default.
    • LLCs can elect S-Corp or C-Corp taxation.
    • LLCs are flexible for adding members later.

    LLC Formation Steps¶

    • Choose a business name and check availability.
    • File Articles of Organization with your state.
    • Draft an Operating Agreement, even for single-member LLCs.
    • Obtain an EIN from the IRS.
    • Open a business bank account and separate finances.
    • Apply for required licenses or permits.

    LLC Tax Treatment (Default)¶

    • LLC profit is reported on Schedule C (single-member).
    • Net income is subject to income tax and self-employment tax.
    • You can take the QBI deduction if eligible.
    • You can deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses.

    LLC Pros¶

    • Simple setup and low annual maintenance in many states.
    • Strong liability separation when formalities are maintained.
    • Flexible profit distribution if multi-member.
    • No payroll requirement for owners.
    • Easy to convert to S-Corp later.

    LLC Cons¶

    • All net income is subject to self-employment tax.
    • QBI phaseouts can reduce benefits at higher income levels.
    • Some states have high LLC fees or franchise taxes.

    S-Corp Basics¶

    • S-Corp is a tax election, not a separate entity.
    • You can elect S-Corp tax status for an LLC or corporation.
    • Profits pass through to owners like a partnership.
    • Owners who work in the business must receive payroll.
    • Profit beyond salary is distributed without SE tax.

    S-Corp Eligibility Requirements¶

    • Must be a US entity (LLC or corporation).
    • Max 100 shareholders.
    • Only allowable shareholders (US citizens/resident individuals).
    • One class of stock (economic rights equal).

    S-Corp Election Steps¶

    • Form an LLC or corporation under state law.
    • Obtain an EIN if you do not already have one.
    • File Form 2553 with the IRS by the deadline.
    • Set up payroll (including state payroll accounts).
    • File annual payroll tax forms (941, W-2, etc.).

    S-Corp Payroll Basics¶

    • Owner-employees must receive reasonable compensation.
    • Salary is subject to income tax and payroll tax.
    • Distributions are not subject to payroll tax.
    • You must withhold and remit payroll taxes.
    • Payroll providers simplify compliance.

    Reasonable Compensation Factors¶

    • Comparable market rates for security consultants.
    • Your billable hours and profit margin.
    • Your role (technical delivery vs sales/management).
    • Client complexity and regulatory requirements.
    • Consistency with what you pay other staff.

    S-Corp Pros¶

    • Potential savings on self-employment taxes.
    • Clear separation between salary and distributions.
    • Credibility with enterprise clients.
    • Can support retirement plan contributions via payroll.

    S-Corp Cons¶

    • Payroll setup and ongoing compliance costs.
    • Additional tax filings (Form 1120-S, K-1s, payroll forms).
    • Risk of IRS scrutiny for low salary.
    • State-level S-Corp taxes or fees may apply.

    LLC vs S-Corp Decision Triggers¶

    • Net profit consistently exceeds $60,000 to $80,000.
    • You are comfortable running payroll.
    • You want to optimize SE tax without aggressive positions.
    • You plan to hire employees or regular contractors.
    • You expect stable revenue and predictable cash flow.

    Comparison Table (Simplified)¶

    • Formation complexity: LLC low, S-Corp medium.
    • Ongoing cost: LLC low, S-Corp medium to high.
    • SE tax on profit: LLC yes, S-Corp partial.
    • Payroll required: LLC no, S-Corp yes.
    • Flexibility for ownership: LLC high, S-Corp medium.
    • Investor compatibility: LLC medium, S-Corp low.

    Decision Flow (Plain Language)¶

    • If you are starting out, form an LLC.
    • If profit is inconsistent, stay with LLC taxation.
    • If profit is steady and high, elect S-Corp.
    • If you plan to bring in non-US owners, avoid S-Corp.
    • If state fees are high, re-evaluate the savings.

    State and Local Considerations¶

    • Some states tax LLCs with annual franchise fees.
    • Some states do not recognize S-Corp elections.
    • City-level business taxes can apply to services.
    • Local gross receipts taxes can reduce savings.

    Banking and Accounting Setup¶

    • Open a dedicated business checking account.
    • Use a business credit card for expenses.
    • Separate personal and business finances at all times.
    • Pick accounting software early (e.g., QuickBooks, Xero).
    • Set a monthly bookkeeping cadence.

    Estimated Taxes Overview¶

    • The US tax system is pay-as-you-go.
    • Consultants must pay quarterly estimated taxes.
    • Estimated taxes cover income tax and SE/payroll taxes.
    • Failure to pay can trigger penalties.

    Quarterly Estimated Tax Dates¶

    • Q1 payment: April 15.
    • Q2 payment: June 15.
    • Q3 payment: September 15.
    • Q4 payment: January 15 of the next year.

    Safe Harbor Rules¶

    • Pay 90% of current year tax, or
    • Pay 100% of prior year tax (110% for high income).
    • Safe harbor helps avoid penalties if income fluctuates.

    Estimated Tax Calculation Steps¶

    • Estimate gross receipts for the year.
    • Subtract deductible business expenses.
    • Calculate net profit.
    • Apply SE tax or payroll tax as appropriate.
    • Apply income tax brackets.
    • Divide into quarterly payments.

    Example: LLC Estimated Taxes (Simplified)¶

    • Gross receipts: $120,000.
    • Expenses: $30,000.
    • Net profit: $90,000.
    • SE tax estimate: ~$12,700 (approx 15.3%).
    • Taxable income after SE adjustment: ~$83,650.
    • Federal income tax estimate: depends on filing status.
    • Add state income tax estimate as applicable.

    Example: S-Corp Estimated Taxes (Simplified)¶

    • Gross receipts: $120,000.
    • Expenses: $30,000.
    • Net profit: $90,000.
    • Reasonable salary: $55,000.
    • Payroll taxes on salary: ~$8,400 (combined).
    • Distributions: ~$35,000 (no SE tax).
    • Income tax still applies to total pass-through income.

    Estimated Tax Payment Methods¶

    • IRS Direct Pay for individuals.
    • EFTPS for businesses.
    • State revenue portal for state estimates.
    • Set calendar reminders for each quarter.

    Deductions Overview¶

    • Deduct ordinary and necessary business expenses.
    • Keep receipts and a clear business purpose.
    • Use a separate account for reimbursable expenses.
    • Track mileage and travel in real time.

    Common Deductions for Security Consultants¶

    • Professional liability (E&O) insurance.
    • General liability insurance.
    • Cybersecurity tooling and subscriptions.
    • Domain names and hosting for client portals.
    • Cloud lab environments and test infrastructure.
    • Laptop, monitor, and security hardware tools.
    • Training, certifications, and conferences.
    • Books, subscriptions, and research materials.
    • Phone and internet (business portion).
    • Business travel, lodging, and airfare.
    • Meals during business travel (50% deductible).
    • Client meetings meals (50% deductible).
    • Marketing, website, and branding costs.
    • Professional fees (CPA, attorney).
    • Payment processing fees.
    • Office supplies.
    • Software licenses and SaaS subscriptions.

    Home Office Deduction¶

    • Must be used regularly and exclusively for business.
    • Two methods: simplified or actual expenses.
    • Simplified: $5 per square foot up to 300 sq ft.
    • Actual: allocate rent, utilities, and insurance.
    • Document square footage and usage.

    Vehicle and Travel Deductions¶

    • Standard mileage or actual expenses method.
    • Keep a mileage log with dates and purposes.
    • Commuting is not deductible.
    • Business travel requires a business purpose.
    • Split personal vs business travel costs.

    Equipment and Depreciation¶

    • Equipment may be depreciated over time.
    • Section 179 allows immediate expensing.
    • Bonus depreciation may be available.
    • Keep purchase receipts and asset logs.

    Education and Training¶

    • Continuing education is generally deductible.
    • Certifications like OSCP or CISSP may qualify.
    • Training must maintain or improve existing skills.
    • New trade or business training may be limited.

    Retirement Contributions (Overview)¶

    • Contributions reduce taxable income.
    • Choices depend on entity and payroll setup.
    • Plan administration fees may apply.

    Retirement Options for LLC¶

    • SEP IRA: simple setup, high contribution limits.
    • Solo 401(k): allows employee + employer contributions.
    • Traditional or Roth IRA: lower limits but easy setup.

    Retirement Options for S-Corp¶

    • Solo 401(k) with payroll contributions.
    • SEP IRA based on W-2 wages.
    • Employer match structure can be designed.

    Health Insurance Deductions¶

    • Self-employed health insurance deduction may apply.
    • Must have net profit to claim.
    • S-Corp owners >2% have special reporting rules.

    QBI Deduction Notes¶

    • QBI can be up to 20% of qualified income.
    • Specified service trade rules can phase out at higher income.
    • W-2 wage and basis limitations can apply.
    • Work with a CPA to validate eligibility.

    Sales Tax Considerations¶

    • Most professional services are not subject to sales tax.
    • Some states tax digital or consulting services.
    • Confirm requirements with your state tax authority.

    International Payment Considerations¶

    • Foreign clients may require different tax forms.
    • Determine if services are US-source or foreign-source.
    • US consultants usually pay US tax on global income.
    • Keep documentation for foreign tax credits if applicable.

    Collecting Tax Forms from Foreign Clients¶

    • Provide a W-9 for US-based clients.
    • For foreign clients, you might provide a W-8BEN.
    • If you are a US person, you generally use a W-9.
    • Some foreign clients require vendor onboarding forms.

    Receiving Payments from Abroad¶

    • Use reputable payment providers.
    • Consider wire fees and FX spreads.
    • Decide if you will invoice in USD or local currency.
    • Document exchange rates on invoice date.

    VAT/GST and Foreign Taxes¶

    • Some countries withhold tax on services.
    • Check for treaty benefits to reduce withholding.
    • Request a withholding certificate if tax is withheld.
    • You may claim a foreign tax credit on your US return.

    OFAC and Sanctions Screening¶

    • Avoid transactions with sanctioned parties.
    • Keep a basic screening process for new clients.
    • Document your screening steps.

    Data Privacy and Cross-Border Work¶

    • GDPR may apply if handling EU data.
    • Use proper data processing agreements if required.
    • Ensure contracts address data transfer and security.

    Contractor Agreements Overview¶

    • Written agreements reduce misclassification risk.
    • Define scope, payment, IP ownership, and confidentiality.
    • Clarify that the contractor controls work methods.
    • Avoid treating contractors like employees.

    Core Clauses for Contractor Agreements¶

    • Scope of work and deliverables.
    • Payment terms and invoicing schedule.
    • Independent contractor status language.
    • Confidentiality and non-disclosure obligations.
    • IP ownership and work-for-hire provisions.
    • Data security and handling requirements.
    • Limitation of liability where possible.
    • Insurance requirements (E&O or general liability).
    • Subcontracting permissions or restrictions.
    • Termination rights and notice periods.

    Statement of Work (SOW) Elements¶

    • Project objectives and success criteria.
    • Detailed deliverables and milestones.
    • Testing methodology and scope boundaries.
    • Client responsibilities and access requirements.
    • Reporting format and deadlines.

    Payment Terms for Contractors¶

    • Net 15 or net 30 terms are common.
    • Define late payment fees if desired.
    • Specify reimbursement rules for expenses.
    • Require itemized invoices with dates and tasks.

    Contractor Classification Checklist¶

    • Contractor controls schedule and methods.
    • Contractor supplies their own tools.
    • Contractor can work for multiple clients.
    • Contractor is paid per project or milestone.
    • You do not provide employee benefits.

    Client Agreements Essentials¶

    • Master Services Agreement (MSA) plus SOWs.
    • Define scope boundaries to prevent scope creep.
    • Clarify testing authorization and rules of engagement.
    • Include safe harbor language for security testing.

    Liability and Risk Management¶

    • Use limitation of liability clauses.
    • Exclude consequential or indirect damages.
    • Specify maximum liability, often tied to fees paid.
    • Require client backups before testing.

    Confidentiality and Data Security¶

    • Define confidential information clearly.
    • Require secure handling and storage of data.
    • Use encryption for sensitive deliverables.
    • Establish incident notification processes.

    IP Ownership for Security Work¶

    • Specify ownership of custom scripts and reports.
    • Consider licensing reusable tools to clients.
    • Clarify open-source use in deliverables.

    Non-Solicit and Non-Compete¶

    • Keep restrictions reasonable in time and scope.
    • Some states restrict non-compete enforcement.
    • Focus on non-solicit of client staff or leads.

    Dispute Resolution¶

    • Define governing law and venue.
    • Consider arbitration for cross-border clients.
    • Include attorneys' fees clause if appropriate.

    Insurance Considerations¶

    • General liability for business operations.
    • Professional liability (E&O) for service mistakes.
    • Cyber liability for data incidents.
    • Workers' comp may be required with employees.

    Business Licenses and Compliance¶

    • Check state and city business license requirements.
    • Some industries require security-specific licensing.
    • Maintain good standing with annual filings.

    Corporate Transparency Act (BOI)¶

    • Many entities must file Beneficial Ownership Information.
    • Filing is required for new entities after 2024.
    • Update filings when ownership changes.
    • Penalties apply for non-compliance.

    Recordkeeping Best Practices¶

    • Save receipts and invoices digitally.
    • Track income by client and project.
    • Reconcile accounts monthly.
    • Keep copies of signed agreements.
    • Retain tax records for at least 7 years.

    Monthly Close Checklist¶

    • Reconcile bank and credit card statements.
    • Review open invoices and follow up.
    • Categorize expenses and attach receipts.
    • Review contractor invoices for accuracy.
    • Update mileage log.
    • Set aside tax reserves.

    Tax Reserve Strategy¶

    • Separate tax savings account.
    • Set aside 25% to 35% of net income.
    • Adjust percentage based on actual tax rate.
    • Revisit after each quarterly payment.

    Pricing and Cash Flow Tips¶

    • Use retainer agreements for stable cash flow.
    • Require deposits for fixed-fee work.
    • Align payment milestones with deliverables.
    • Offer ACH discounts to reduce card fees.

    International Client Invoice Checklist¶

    • Use clear currency and payment terms.
    • Include bank details and SWIFT/IBAN.
    • State who covers wire fees.
    • Reference any withholding or tax obligations.
    • Provide tax residency documentation if requested.

    Security-Specific Clauses¶

    • Define authorized IP ranges and targets.
    • Clarify social engineering scope.
    • Require written authorization for exploitation.
    • Describe data retention and deletion timelines.
    • Provide rules for vulnerability disclosure.

    Subcontractor Management¶

    • Verify contractor certifications and background.
    • Use NDAs for subcontractors.
    • Ensure contractor insurance if needed.
    • Confirm contractor tax forms before payment.

    1099 Compliance¶

    • Collect W-9 from US contractors.
    • Issue 1099-NEC for payments >= $600.
    • Track payments by vendor to simplify filing.

    Payroll Compliance for S-Corp¶

    • Register for state payroll tax accounts.
    • Withhold federal and state income tax.
    • File quarterly payroll returns (Form 941).
    • Issue W-2s and file W-3 annually.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid¶

    • Mixing personal and business funds.
    • Failing to pay quarterly estimated taxes.
    • Underpaying reasonable compensation in S-Corp.
    • Missing state filing deadlines.
    • Ignoring contract scope limitations.

    When to Hire a CPA¶

    • Prior-year tax surprises or penalties.
    • S-Corp election or payroll setup.
    • Multi-state clients and sales tax questions.
    • International payment complexity.

    When to Hire an Attorney¶

    • Drafting or negotiating MSAs and SOWs.
    • Disputes over scope or liability.
    • IP ownership or licensing strategy.
    • Employment or contractor classification issues.

    Example: Simple LLC Year-End Tax Checklist¶

    • Reconcile all accounts through December 31.
    • Review open invoices for collectability.
    • Confirm all 1099 vendors are captured.
    • Verify asset purchases for depreciation.
    • Export profit and loss statement.
    • Prepare Schedule C support data.

    Example: S-Corp Year-End Tax Checklist¶

    • Confirm payroll totals match salary policy.
    • Reconcile shareholder distributions.
    • Confirm payroll forms filed (941, W-2, W-3).
    • Prepare Form 1120-S and K-1s.
    • Review reasonable compensation documentation.

    Decision Scenarios¶

    • Scenario A: New consultant with sporadic income.
    • Recommended: LLC with default taxation.
    • Scenario B: Stable $120k profit with low expenses.
    • Recommended: Consider S-Corp election.
    • Scenario C: Plan to add foreign partner.
    • Recommended: LLC or partnership, not S-Corp.

    Timeline for First Year¶

    • Month 1: Form LLC, open bank accounts.
    • Month 1: Draft MSA and standard SOW.
    • Month 2: Implement bookkeeping system.
    • Month 3: First quarterly tax payment.
    • Month 6: Review profit and S-Corp savings.
    • Month 9: Engage CPA if needed.
    • Month 12: Year-end tax prep and 1099s.

    Annual Compliance Calendar¶

    • January: Issue 1099-NEC forms.
    • January: File W-2s and W-3 if S-Corp.
    • March: File 1120-S and K-1s.
    • April: File personal return or extension.
    • Throughout: Quarterly estimated tax payments.

    International Contracting Red Flags¶

    • Client requests payment to third parties.
    • Client refuses to sign a services agreement.
    • Client operates in sanctioned jurisdictions.
    • Client asks for results without scope boundaries.

    Data Handling and Retention¶

    • Define report retention period in contracts.
    • Use encrypted storage for reports.
    • Delete client data after agreed period.
    • Document destruction methods for audit readiness.

    Payment Processor Considerations¶

    • Compare ACH fees vs card fees.
    • Ensure processor supports international wires.
    • Track chargebacks and dispute processes.
    • Reconcile processor payouts monthly.

    Marketing and Lead Generation Deductions¶

    • Website design and hosting.
    • Content creation and SEO tools.
    • Paid ads and sponsorships.
    • Professional networking memberships.

    Security Tools Deduction Examples¶

    • Vulnerability scanners and testing suites.
    • Security lab subscriptions.
    • Code signing certificates.
    • Threat intelligence feeds.

    Travel and Conference Planning¶

    • Document business purpose for each trip.
    • Keep agendas and receipts for conferences.
    • Track which meals are deductible.
    • Separate personal days from business days.

    Multi-State Work Considerations¶

    • Some states require registration for doing business.
    • Nexus can be created by in-person work.
    • You may need to file nonresident returns.
    • Seek state guidance for complex cases.

    Pricing for Tax Coverage¶

    • Model pricing to cover tax obligations.
    • Include time for non-billable admin work.
    • Factor in insurance and compliance costs.

    Example Tax Reserve Calculation¶

    • Net monthly income after expenses: $7,000.
    • Set aside 30%: $2,100 per month.
    • Reserve for quarterly payment: $6,300.

    Sample Contractor Onboarding Checklist¶

    • Signed contractor agreement.
    • Completed W-9 form.
    • Proof of insurance (if required).
    • Security policy acknowledgment.
    • NDA and confidentiality agreement.

    Sample Client Onboarding Checklist¶

    • Signed MSA and SOW.
    • Confirmation of testing authorization.
    • Access credentials and IPs.
    • Points of contact for incident response.
    • Billing contact and invoice details.

    Template: Invoicing Essentials¶

    • Client legal name and address.
    • Invoice number and date.
    • Description of services and scope.
    • Payment terms and due date.
    • Late fee policy if applicable.

    Cash vs Accrual Accounting¶

    • Cash basis is common for small businesses.
    • Accrual may be required at higher scale.
    • Choose a method and stay consistent.

    Foreign Contractor Payments¶

    • Collect W-8BEN or W-8BEN-E as appropriate.
    • Consider US withholding requirements in specific cases.
    • Track payments in USD equivalent.
    • Keep proof of foreign status for audits.

    Subcontractor IP and Confidentiality¶

    • Ensure subcontractors assign IP to your company.
    • Require confidentiality obligations to match client terms.
    • Flow down key contract restrictions to subs.

    Security Compliance Standards¶

    • Some clients require SOC 2 or ISO 27001 alignment.
    • Document your internal security policies.

    FAQs¶

    • Q: Can I be an LLC and S-Corp at the same time?
    • A: Yes, an LLC can elect S-Corp taxation.
    • Q: Do I need payroll if I am the only owner?
    • A: If S-Corp elected and you work in the business, yes.
    • Q: Is all income taxed even if I leave cash in the LLC?
    • A: Generally yes, pass-through income is taxed to owners.
    • Q: Can I pay contractors internationally without a W-8?
    • A: You should collect a W-8 to document foreign status.
