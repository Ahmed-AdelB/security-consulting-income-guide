    • Purpose: translate repo research into an education-sector security consulting guide.
    • Sources: only repository files with education/training references and general consulting data.
    • No FERPA references were found in the repository.
    • No K-12 or higher-education market size figures were found in the repository.
    • "10K" label reflects document scale, not a verified 10k-source bibliography.
    • Quantitative data below is non-sector-specific unless labeled education.
    • Use this as a structured research notebook until external sources are added.
    • Validate pricing data against current market conditions.
    • Validate compliance interpretations with legal counsel.
    • This draft separates extracted facts from hypotheses.

    3. Education Sector Segmentation (Hypotheses)¶

    3.1 K-12 environment hypotheses (validate with stakeholders)¶

    • Governance often centers on district leadership and school boards.
    • Budget cycles are typically annual and tied to fiscal-year planning.
    • Funding may include state allocations, federal grants, and local levies.
    • IT staffing is often lean with shared responsibilities.
    • Device fleets may include 1:1 student devices and shared labs.
    • Classroom tech may include smartboards, projectors, and teacher laptops.
    • Remote learning tooling may expand the attack surface.
    • Seasonal peaks occur around semester starts and standardized testing.
    • Network segmentation may separate administrative, student, and guest traffic.
    • Incident response capacity may be constrained by staffing and tooling.
    • Vendor sprawl may include edtech SaaS with limited security review.
    • Third-party access may include curriculum vendors and support providers.
    • Data protection focus includes student privacy and safeguarding.
    • Authentication may rely on shared or simplified credentials in some grades.
    • Email phishing defense is critical due to staff turnover and busy cycles.
    • Identity lifecycle includes frequent enrollments and graduations.
    • Compliance burden varies by state and district policies.

    3.2 Higher-education environment hypotheses (validate with stakeholders)¶

    • Governance may be decentralized across colleges, departments, and labs.
    • IT may operate federated identity and delegated admin models.
    • Research networks may require open access for collaboration.
    • Campus networks may include dorms, guest networks, and IoT.
    • Faculty and staff device diversity increases endpoint complexity.
    • Student populations create high-volume account provisioning cycles.
    • Third-party contracts may include research partners and consortia.
    • Cloud adoption may be decentralized across departments.
    • Data types include research data, IP, and student records.
    • Security expectations may vary between central IT and research groups.
    • Shadow IT may be common due to autonomy and grant-funded projects.
    • Incident response may require coordination with legal and research offices.
    • Compliance expectations may include multi-regulatory exposure.
    • International students and partnerships may add data transfer complexities.
    • Alumni systems and donor platforms add additional sensitive data sets.

    3.3 Shared education-sector characteristics (validate with stakeholders)¶

    • High user churn due to enrollments, graduations, and staff turnover.
    • Diverse device ecosystems (student, faculty, staff, guest).
    • Mixed maturity across security controls.
    • Dependence on SaaS platforms for learning and collaboration.
    • Remote access and VPN usage during off-campus learning.
    • High sensitivity to downtime due to class schedules and exams.
    • Reputation risk for data breaches involving minors or student records.
    • Budget pressure limits the ability to hire specialized security staff.
    • Multi-site environments with varying network architectures.
    • Opportunity for managed services and shared security tooling.

    • Define what qualifies as an education record.
    • Map directory information rules and opt-out handling.
    • Define consent requirements for disclosure.
    • Clarify parent versus student rights by age or grade.
    • Identify breach notification obligations under FERPA.
    • Document vendor contractual obligations under FERPA.
    • Clarify data sharing with third-party edtech vendors.
    • Map retention and destruction expectations for education records.
    • Define audit requirements for FERPA compliance.
    • Identify intersections with state student privacy laws.

    6.2 Education pricing input checklist¶

    • Number of sites (schools, campuses, admin buildings).
    • Number of wireless access points per site.
    • Number of users (students, staff, faculty).
    • Number of critical systems (SIS, LMS, finance).
    • Required compliance frameworks (PCI, HIPAA, NIST, ISO).
    • Desired reporting depth (executive vs technical).
    • Required on-site vs remote work.
    • Required turnaround timelines.
    • Need for training or tabletop exercises.

    9. Risk Scenarios and Control Themes (Hypotheses)¶

    9.1 Common education-sector risk scenarios to validate¶

    • Phishing leading to credential compromise.
    • Ransomware impacting student information systems.
    • Unauthorized access to student records.
    • Data leakage through misconfigured cloud storage.
    • Compromised teacher or admin accounts.
    • Third-party vendor breach affecting student data.
    • Wireless network misuse via rogue access points.
    • Lost or stolen devices with sensitive data.
    • Disruption of testing or exam platforms.
    • Denial of service impacting online learning portals.

    9.2 Control themes to assess¶

    16. Appendix D: Incident Response Checklist¶

    • Confirm incident detection source and timestamp.
    • Validate incident scope and affected systems.
    • Engage incident commander and response team.
    • Preserve evidence and logs.
    • Isolate affected systems if needed.
    • Confirm backups and recovery options.
    • Notify leadership and legal counsel.
    • Determine regulatory notification requirements.
    • Engage external IR vendor if needed.
    • Document containment actions taken.
    • Reset credentials for compromised accounts.
    • Review MFA enforcement and anomalies.
    • Patch exploited vulnerabilities.
    • Scan for lateral movement indicators.
    • Validate data exfiltration indicators.
    • Coordinate communications with PR teams.
    • Notify insurers if cyber coverage exists.
    • Track incident timeline and decisions.
    • Collect forensic images if required.
    • Validate restoration from clean backups.
    • Monitor for re-compromise after remediation.
    • Provide interim status updates to leadership.
    • Draft final incident report.
    • Conduct post-incident lessons learned review.
    • Update security controls based on findings.
    • Review vendor responsibilities and SLAs.
    • Update policies and runbooks.
    • Plan follow-up testing to validate fixes.
    • Provide training on new lessons learned.
    • Update risk register with incident findings.
    • Close incident with executive sign-off.
