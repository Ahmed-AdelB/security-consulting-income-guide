    Scope, Constraints, and Methodology¶

    Scope This research focuses on sponsorship content for security podcasts, with two anchor case studies: Darknet Diaries (long-form narrative) and Risky Business (weekly security news and analysis). It also includes broader market context, sponsorship models, messaging frameworks, and practical templates for ad reads and media kits.

    Constraints - No live web access is available in this environment. - Specific details such as current sponsors, rates, or live download numbers may have changed. - The analysis relies on generally known patterns in podcast advertising and security media. - All show-specific facts should be verified before use in commercial materials.

    Methodology The document synthesizes: - Common podcast monetization models across B2B and technical audiences. - Best practices for host-read sponsorship content. - Audience and buyer persona analysis for security products and services. - Known characteristics of Darknet Diaries and Risky Business as flagship security podcasts.

    Executive Summary¶

    • Security podcasts are trust-centric: credibility, technical specificity, and authenticity matter more than flashy marketing.
    • The most effective sponsorships for technical audiences are host-read and include tangible proof points (benchmarks, case studies, outcomes).
    • Darknet Diaries is best suited for narrative alignment and evergreen storytelling; Risky Business is best suited for timely messages and enterprise decision-makers.
    • B2B security sponsors often succeed with ads that are educational and pragmatic rather than fear-based.
    • Sponsorship pricing for niche technical shows tends to be higher CPM but lower total inventory; sponsorship “value” often hinges on audience seniority and purchase influence.
    • Long-form security podcasts benefit from “baked-in” ads that stay with episodes, while news-driven shows can capitalize on dynamic ad insertion for rapid messaging updates.
    • A high-trust sponsorship strategy blends short-term conversion goals (demo requests, event signups) with long-term brand lift (awareness among practitioners and CISOs).
    • Risk and compliance considerations are elevated in security; ad reads should avoid misleading claims, include disclaimers, and respect lawful use.
    • Successful security sponsorships often include extensions beyond audio: newsletter placements, webinar co-hosting, or security report co-branding.
    • The strongest sponsor–show relationships are multi-episode commitments that allow message iteration and product education over time.

    Security Podcast Sponsorship Landscape¶

    Cybersecurity podcasts occupy a distinct niche between mainstream tech shows and enterprise media. Their audiences tend to be more technical, more skeptical, and more attentive to substance. Many listeners are security practitioners (engineers, analysts, incident responders), but there is also a meaningful segment of managers and executive-level stakeholders who consume podcasts as part of their weekly “signal intake.”

    Security media is fragmented. Many shows are independent or host-led, which increases authenticity but limits ad inventory. In this environment, sponsorships are less about mass reach and more about targeted access to a high-intent audience. The audiences also tend to be global and diverse, which impacts messaging (avoid US-only offers unless explicitly stated) and compliance (GDPR and privacy concerns can be particularly salient).

    The security category has two dominant monetization patterns: 1. Host-read sponsor segments integrated into the show’s narrative flow. 2. Network or media bundle sponsorships where podcasts are part of a larger content ecosystem that includes newsletters, webinars, reports, and conferences.

    In both cases, the key differentiator is trust. Security professionals distrust overhyped marketing and are sensitive to misleading claims. If the sponsor can demonstrate real-world credibility—e.g., specific incident response experience, public benchmarks, or a strong community reputation—the ad will convert better and can even become part of the “signal” listeners appreciate.

    Another characteristic of security podcasts: the line between news and narrative. Some shows follow a narrative storytelling model (e.g., a single incident or hacker story) that lends itself to evergreen content. Others are strictly news-driven and benefit from timely, frequently updated sponsorship messages. These two formats require different sponsorship strategies.

    Narrative shows: - Tend to have longer shelf life; episodes are re-discovered years later. - Sponsorship value may be higher if ads remain baked-in. - Audiences are emotionally engaged, which can amplify sponsor recall if ads are contextually relevant.

    News and analysis shows: - Emphasize timeliness and topical alignment. - Sponsorship value often higher for quick calls to action (events, webinars, product launches). - Dynamic ad insertion is common, enabling rapid campaign changes.

    The best security sponsorships often blend both: a primary sponsorship in a flagship show plus an add-on in a shorter news brief or newsletter. This helps capture both brand lift and conversion-focused actions.

    Audience and Buyer Persona Profiles¶

    Security podcast audiences are not monolithic. Below are common personas that influence sponsorship outcomes. These personas matter because they affect how you design sponsor messaging, calls to action, and follow-on nurturing.

    Persona 1: Security Engineer / Analyst (Practitioner)¶

    • Primary goals: Solve real security problems; learn new tools; reduce manual work; prevent incidents.
    • Pain points: Alert fatigue; limited budget; complex tooling; noisy marketing.
    • Decision role: Influencer. Often advocates for tools but may not hold final budget authority.
    • Message resonance: Technical details, hands-on demos, use cases, tool integration, proof-of-concepts.
    • Preferred CTA: Free trial, sandbox access, open-source tools, or a detailed technical guide.

    Persona 2: Security Manager / Lead¶

    • Primary goals: Build reliable security programs; improve team efficiency; meet compliance obligations.
    • Pain points: Staffing shortages; team burnout; tooling sprawl; budget scrutiny.
    • Decision role: Strong influencer or co-decision maker.
    • Message resonance: ROI framing, operational efficiency, incident response speed, simplified stack.
    • Preferred CTA: Case study, security benchmark report, or demo with measurable outcomes.

    Persona 3: CISO / VP Security¶

    • Primary goals: Risk reduction, strategic alignment, and board reporting.
    • Pain points: Budget justification; vendor due diligence; reputational risk.
    • Decision role: Final decision or executive sponsor.
    • Message resonance: Strategic outcomes, total cost reduction, reduction in breach impact, governance support.
    • Preferred CTA: Executive brief, peer reference, or a short whitepaper on risk mitigation.

    Persona 4: Security Researcher / Specialist¶

    • Primary goals: Deep technical exploration, exploitation, and defense innovations.
    • Pain points: Lack of transparency; “black box” products; false claims.
    • Decision role: Influencer; sometimes external advisor or community leader.
    • Message resonance: Research validation, open telemetry, proof of efficacy, technical transparency.
    • Preferred CTA: Technical blog, research report, open dataset or benchmark.

    Persona 5: Developer / DevOps / Platform Engineer¶

    • Primary goals: Secure code, automation, minimal friction in deployment.
    • Pain points: Security controls slowing delivery; compliance overhead.
    • Decision role: Influencer, sometimes owner of tool adoption in dev-centric environments.
    • Message resonance: Integration with CI/CD, developer-friendly workflows, fast onboarding.
    • Preferred CTA: “Get started in 15 minutes” guide or a CLI tutorial.

    Understanding these personas helps when crafting sponsorship content. For example, Darknet Diaries listeners skew toward practitioners and curious technologists, while Risky Business often includes decision makers, security managers, and vendor watchers. Your creative should map to those roles.

    Sponsorship Inventory and Formats¶

    Security podcasts typically offer a mix of the following inventory types. The best sponsorships are aligned with the show’s format and the sponsor’s desired outcomes.

    1) Host-Read Ad Spots¶

    • Pre-roll: 15–30 seconds. Best for brand awareness and short CTAs.
    • Mid-roll: 30–90 seconds. Best for deeper messaging and product education.
    • Post-roll: 10–20 seconds. Lower recall but can support repetition.

    Host-read ads are especially effective in security podcasts because hosts are seen as credible practitioners or journalists. A mid-roll ad in a narrative show can perform like an endorsement if the message fits the audience’s expectations.

    2) Sponsored Segments or “Billboards”¶

    Short sponsor mentions at the top or during transitions. Often used to recognize the sponsor without a full ad read.

    3) Dynamic Ad Insertion (DAI)¶

    Allows replacing ads in back catalog episodes. Effective for time-sensitive campaigns but can reduce “evergreen” endorsements. Some sponsors prefer baked-in ads to capture the long-tail audience.

    4) Sponsored Interviews or Bonus Episodes¶

    Used carefully. The security audience is skeptical, so full sponsored episodes should be clearly labeled and still deliver value. These can be excellent for deep product education when executed transparently.

    5) Newsletter and Web Tie-ins¶

    Many security podcasts run newsletters or websites. Sponsorship packages may include newsletter slots, landing page placements, and show notes links.

    6) Live Events, Webinars, and Virtual Summits¶

    Some shows host virtual events or partner with sponsors for expert sessions. These can be powerful for lead generation when the content is substantive (e.g., incident response drills, tabletop exercises).

    7) Community and Slack/Discord Sponsorships¶

    Security communities are active and often invite sponsor engagement, but authenticity is crucial. Sponsors must contribute meaningfully rather than “selling.”

    Pricing, Packaging, and Negotiation¶

    Podcast sponsorship rates vary widely, and in the security niche, pricing is often influenced by audience seniority and B2B product value rather than raw download counts. Since live figures are unavailable here, use these as directional guidelines only.

    Common Pricing Models¶

    • CPM (cost per thousand downloads): Often in the $25–$100+ range for niche B2B audiences, sometimes higher for highly targeted shows.
    • Flat rate per episode: Common in smaller or independent shows with predictable sponsor slots.
    • Seasonal bundle: Multi-episode packages that guarantee a consistent sponsor presence.
    • Network bundle: Cross-show packages (podcast + newsletter + webinar) with a single negotiated price.

    Packaging Principles¶

    • Multi-episode commitments outperform one-offs. Repetition builds trust and allows message refinement.
    • Mid-roll inventory tends to be premium, especially for long-form shows.
    • Story or topic adjacency can be valuable. Example: sponsoring an episode on ransomware with a response platform.
    • Additional assets like website banners, show notes links, or social posts should be negotiated if they provide real traffic.

    Negotiation Tips for Sponsors¶

    • Ask for audience demographics and listener surveys rather than relying solely on download numbers.
    • If the show is evergreen, negotiate for “baked-in” placements in new episodes and limited DAI for back catalog.
    • Request a pre-flight or test ad read to align tone and technical accuracy.
    • Clarify ad placement (pre/mid/post), length, and whether the host will personalize the read.

    Measurement, Attribution, and ROI¶

    Security marketing attribution is challenging due to long sales cycles and multi-touch journeys. Sponsorship measurement should include both direct response and brand lift indicators.

    Direct Response Tracking¶

    • Unique URLs (e.g., example.com/podcast) that are easy to remember and type.
    • UTM parameters for podcast-specific campaigns.
    • Dedicated landing pages with tailored messaging for podcast listeners.
    • Unique promo codes or offer codes (less common in B2B but still useful for tracking).

    Indirect / Brand Lift Signals¶

    • Increases in branded search queries after episode release.
    • Spikes in website direct traffic during campaign windows.
    • Growth in inbound inquiries that mention the podcast unprompted.
    • Social engagement from known security practitioners or organizations.

    Sales Cycle Considerations¶

    Security tools often have long evaluation phases. This means the primary value of podcast sponsorship may be top-of-funnel awareness rather than immediate conversion. Sponsors should align expectations accordingly and use marketing automation to nurture leads captured via podcast CTAs.

    Practical Measurement Stack¶

    • CRM attribution: tag leads with “podcast” source when possible.
    • First-party tracking: cookies or session tagging on landing pages.
    • Post-demo surveys: ask “How did you hear about us?” with a podcast option.

    Brand Safety, Compliance, and Trust¶

    Security marketing is uniquely sensitive. Sponsors are often in industries where trust is paramount, and the podcast audience will punish misleading or sensational claims.

    FTC Disclosure and Transparency¶

    Host-read ads should be clearly labeled as sponsored. The tone can be conversational, but the sponsor relationship should not be ambiguous.

    Claims and Proof¶

    Avoid unverified claims like “prevents all breaches” or “stops every attack.” Instead, focus on measurable outcomes such as “reduces mean time to detect” or “helps teams prioritize critical alerts.”

    Lawful Use and Ethical Messaging¶

    Some security products can be dual-use (defensive and offensive). Ad copy should emphasize ethical, legal use cases and avoid promoting misuse.

    Privacy and Data Responsibility¶

    If sponsorships involve data collection (e.g., through landing pages), ensure privacy disclosures are clear and align with security community expectations.

    Security-Specific Sponsorship Content Strategy¶

    Security audiences respond to clear, honest, technically grounded messaging. Here are principles for designing sponsor messages that resonate.

    1) Lead with Problem and Context¶

    Security professionals want to know you understand their pain. Start with a concrete problem: alert fatigue, phishing proliferation, cloud misconfigurations, or incident response delays.

    2) Provide a Credible Technical Hook¶

    Mention specific features or approaches: “behavior-based detection,” “attack path analysis,” “eBPF telemetry,” “asset inventory,” “SOAR playbooks,” etc. Avoid vague claims.

    3) Emphasize Integration and Workflow¶

    Security teams hate tools that create friction. Sponsors should highlight integrations with SIEMs, CI/CD pipelines, cloud providers, or ticketing systems.

    4) Use Real Proof Points¶

    Case studies, benchmark results, or quantified outcomes are powerful. Even a single sentence like “reduced alert volume by 40%” can be effective when credible.

    5) Respect the Audience’s Time¶

    Be concise. If the show allows a 60-second mid-roll, use it to convey the high-level value and a clear CTA, not an exhaustive feature list.

    6) Consider Long-Term Learning Campaigns¶

    In security, repetition and education matter. A multi-episode sponsorship should evolve over time: e.g., episode 1 introduces the problem, episode 2 explains a solution, episode 3 offers a case study.

    7) Avoid Fear, Embrace Empowerment¶

    Fear-based ads can turn off practitioners. Instead, use pragmatic, empowering messaging: “help your team move faster,” “reduce noisy alerts,” “recover more quickly.”

    8) Align With Host Voice¶

    In host-read segments, the host’s tone matters. If the show is narrative and thoughtful, keep the read story-driven. If the show is analytical, keep it direct and data-backed.

    Case Study: Darknet Diaries¶

    Overview¶

    Darknet Diaries is a long-form narrative podcast focused on cybersecurity, cybercrime, and real-world incidents. The show uses story-driven episodes that often explore breaches, hacking campaigns, and security failures. The host’s voice is central and carries a strong sense of credibility and curiosity. This narrative format fosters deep listener engagement and strong recall, which is particularly valuable for sponsors seeking brand affinity and trust.

    Audience Profile (Directional)¶

    • Primary: Security practitioners, hackers, researchers, and tech-curious listeners.
    • Secondary: Managers, CISOs, and technical leaders who use the show for situational awareness.
    • Behavioral pattern: Listeners often binge older episodes, indicating a strong back catalog value.

    Sponsorship Fit¶

    Darknet Diaries is well-suited for sponsors that want to be associated with deep technical storytelling and real-world security lessons. The narrative tone supports sponsorships that feel educational or that provide tools relevant to incident response, threat detection, authentication, or security training.

    Monetization Observations (To Verify)¶

    The following points are directional and should be validated with the show’s media kit. - High confidence: Host-read sponsor segments are a core monetization method. - Medium confidence: Mid-roll spots are likely the premium inventory; pre-rolls also exist. - Medium confidence: Evergreen back catalog adds value; baked-in ads are impactful. - Low confidence: Additional monetization (membership, premium feed, or merch) may exist.

    Sponsorship Content Strategy for Darknet Diaries¶

    Because the show relies on storytelling, sponsor messages work best when they are concise, human, and aligned with the episode’s theme. Ads should avoid jarring shifts in tone. A strong ad here feels like the host is sharing a tool they personally appreciate.

    High-performing message attributes: - A short narrative hook: “I remember the first time I saw X during an incident…” - Clear product category positioning: “endpoint detection,” “identity security,” “backup and recovery.” - One tangible outcome: “reduces time to respond,” “finds assets you didn’t know existed.”

    Example messaging angles (directional): - Incident response readiness: “When the breach happens, speed matters.” - Authentication and identity: “Most breaches start with stolen credentials.” - Security training: “Your people are your most important control.”

    Risks and Sensitivities¶

    • Tone mismatch: Overly commercial language can feel out of place.
    • Overstated claims: The audience is skeptical and may react negatively.
    • Ethical sensitivity: The show often covers victims and breaches; sponsors should avoid appearing to exploit incidents.

    Opportunities for Custom Integrations¶

    • Sponsorship of “special episodes” that dive into a specific domain (e.g., ransomware or insider threats).
    • Co-branded resources like “incident response checklists” or short educational briefings.
    • A narrative-style ad read that aligns with the episode’s story arc.

    Example Darknet Diaries Ad Read (60 Seconds, Narrative Style)¶

    “If you’ve ever sat in the war room during an incident, you know the worst feeling is waiting—waiting for logs, waiting for alerts, waiting for clarity. That’s why this episode is sponsored by [Sponsor], a security platform built for fast, decisive response. It connects signals across your cloud, endpoints, and identity systems, then helps you prioritize what actually matters. Teams use [Sponsor] to cut through noise and reduce the time it takes to contain a breach. If you want to see how it works, check out [short URL] and explore a demo tailored for incident response teams.”

    Case Study: Risky Business¶

    Overview¶

    Risky Business is a long-running security news and analysis podcast, recognized for clear commentary on vulnerabilities, threats, and industry shifts. Its format is typically more direct and analytical than narrative podcasts. The show’s focus on current events makes it especially valuable for sponsors with time-sensitive announcements or research releases.

    Audience Profile (Directional)¶

    • Primary: Security professionals, managers, and decision makers following industry trends.
    • Secondary: Vendors, analysts, and policy watchers seeking broad security context.
    • Behavioral pattern: Regular weekly listeners who expect fresh updates.

    Sponsorship Fit¶

    Risky Business is a strong fit for B2B security companies that want to influence purchasing committees and high-level decision makers. Because the show’s content is current and operational, sponsorships can tie to new reports, product launches, and event invitations.

    Monetization Observations (To Verify)¶

    The following points should be validated directly with the show’s media kit. - High confidence: Host-read sponsor mentions and dedicated sponsor slots are core. - Medium confidence: Additional sponsorships may include newsletter or media bundle options. - Medium confidence: Timely topics make dynamic ad insertion and rapid campaign changes valuable.

    Sponsorship Content Strategy for Risky Business¶

    This show’s tone is professional and analysis-driven. Sponsors benefit by providing concise, credible information that feels like a useful update rather than a sales pitch.

    High-performing message attributes: - Clear framing of a current challenge (e.g., “active exploitation of X”). - A short statement of product capability aligned to that challenge. - CTA focused on a report, webinar, or live demo.

    Example messaging angles: - Threat intelligence: “Stay ahead of campaigns targeting your industry.” - Cloud security posture: “Know where misconfigurations live before they become incidents.” - Vulnerability management: “Prioritize exploitation, not just CVSS scores.”

    Risks and Sensitivities¶

    • Overly long ads can interrupt the flow of news commentary.
    • PR-sounding language can reduce credibility.
    • Lack of specificity can cause sponsors to be ignored.

    Opportunities for Custom Integrations¶

    • Sponsor a special segment on a research topic with clear disclosure.
    • Co-release a security report with host commentary or Q&A.
    • Provide a practical resource (e.g., vulnerability response checklist) tied to the sponsor’s expertise.

    Example Risky Business Ad Read (45 Seconds, Analytical Style)¶

    “This episode is brought to you by [Sponsor]. If you’re tracking today’s exploit landscape, you already know that patch timelines and real-world exploitation don’t always align. [Sponsor] helps security teams prioritize the vulnerabilities that are actually being used in the wild, so you can focus on what matters. Their platform combines threat intel with asset context to surface the highest-risk exposures. To see how it works, grab the latest report at [short URL].”

    Comparative Analysis: Narrative vs. News/Analysis Formats¶

    Dimension Narrative (Darknet Diaries) News/Analysis (Risky Business)
    Primary Value Deep engagement, storytelling Timely insight, actionable updates
    Sponsorship Fit Brand trust, long-term recall Rapid campaigns, product updates
    Best Ad Type Host-read, story-aligned Concise, data-driven
    Shelf Life Long (evergreen) Short (time-sensitive)
    Audience Mindset Immersive, reflective Operational, analytical
    Optimal CTA Demo, resource, guide Report, webinar, product update

    Sponsors can benefit from using both formats. A common strategy is to run a narrative sponsor slot for brand trust and a news sponsor slot for conversion-driven campaigns. This combination helps cover both top-of-funnel awareness and mid-funnel engagement.

    Activation Ideas and Custom Integrations¶

    1. Incident Response Playbook Download: Sponsor provides a checklist or template mentioned in the ad.
    2. Threat Landscape Report: Sponsor releases a short report aligned with an episode topic.
    3. Ask-Me-Anything (AMA): Sponsor offers a Q&A with their security team (carefully moderated).
    4. Tooling Deep Dive Webinar: Co-hosted by sponsor, focusing on practical implementation.
    5. Security Awareness Month Campaign: Sponsor a month-long series with evolving messages.
    6. Career-Focused Sponsorship: Promote training or certification programs.
    7. Incident Simulation Exercise: Sponsor a tabletop exercise summary or guide.
    8. Community Scholarship: Sponsor scholarships for training or conferences.
    9. Open Source Support: Sponsor supports an OSS tool and discusses that contribution.
    10. Security Metrics Benchmark: Sponsor shares anonymized data and findings.
    11. Toolchain Integration Guide: Sponsor provides how-to resources for common stacks.
    12. Bug Bounty Spotlight: Sponsor highlights a responsible disclosure program.
    13. Privacy and Compliance Toolkit: Sponsor offers compliance guidance (with legal disclaimers).
    14. Incident Recovery Story: Sponsor shares a real case study narrative.
    15. “Security Myth” Mini Series: Sponsor debunks common misconceptions with credible info.

    Sponsor Readiness Checklist¶

    Before sponsoring a security podcast, ensure the following are prepared:

    • Clear target persona and desired action (demo, report download, event signup).
    • A dedicated landing page tailored to the podcast audience.
    • Claims that are specific, measurable, and defensible.
    • A short ad script aligned with host tone (narrative vs. analytical).
    • An internal spokesperson who can approve technical accuracy quickly.
    • A post-campaign measurement plan (attribution, lead scoring, brand lift tracking).
    • A follow-up nurture sequence for leads who download resources.
    • A compliance review for data collection and privacy policy alignment.

    Media Kit Request Template and Outreach Plan¶

    Media Kit Request Email (Template)¶

    Subject: Sponsorship Inquiry — [Company] x [Podcast Name]

    Hi [Host/Producer Name],

    I’m reaching out from [Company]. We’re exploring sponsorship opportunities with [Podcast Name] because your audience aligns closely with our security teams and buyers. Could you share your current media kit, including listener demographics, sponsorship inventory, and pricing? We’re also interested in any bundle options (newsletter, webinar, or web placements).

    If helpful, I can share a brief summary of our product and audience goals. Thanks in advance for your time.

    Best, [Name] [Title] [Company]

    Outreach Plan¶

    1. Shortlist shows by audience alignment and format (narrative vs. news).
    2. Request media kit and audience demographics from each show.
    3. Compare offers using a weighted score (audience seniority, alignment, pricing, add-ons).
    4. Pilot with a 2–3 episode sponsorship to refine messaging.
    5. Scale into a multi-episode or quarterly partnership if results are promising.

If you’re building a faster, more resilient security program, take a look at [short URL] and see the demo.”

    Appendix B: ROI Worksheet and Metrics Glossary¶

    ROI Worksheet (Simplified)¶

    • Cost per Episode: Sponsorship fee / number of episodes
    • Total Spend: Cost per episode x number of episodes
    • Attributed Leads: Total leads from podcast landing page
    • Cost per Lead: Total spend / attributed leads
    • Pipeline Influenced: Sum of opportunity value tied to podcast leads
    • ROI: (Pipeline influenced - total spend) / total spend

    Metrics Glossary¶

    • CPM: Cost per thousand downloads.
    • DAI: Dynamic Ad Insertion.
    • Brand Lift: Increased awareness or positive brand perception.
    • MQL: Marketing-qualified lead.
    • SQL: Sales-qualified lead.

    Appendix C: Security Marketing Messaging Cheat Sheet¶

    Phrases that resonate: - “Reduce mean time to detect/respond.” - “Prioritize the vulnerabilities that are actually exploited.” - “Cut alert noise without losing visibility.” - “Integrate with your existing stack in days.” - “Automate routine investigations.”

    Phrases to avoid: - “We stop all breaches.” - “Guaranteed protection.” - “Hack-proof.”

    Helpful positioning cues: - Emphasize operational efficiency over fear. - Show respect for practitioner expertise. - Offer evidence, not hype.

    Appendix D: Risk Questions and Due Diligence¶

    Security sponsorships should include a risk review. Consider these questions before approving a sponsorship deal:

    • Does the sponsor’s product have known controversies that could harm the show’s credibility?
    • Are the sponsor’s claims verifiable and aligned with industry standards?
    • Is the sponsor’s CTA compliant with privacy regulations and data collection norms?
    • Will the ad be clearly labeled as sponsored?
    • Is the sponsor’s product aligned with ethical, legal security practices?
