    M&A Cybersecurity Due Diligence Research (Security Consulting Rates for Acquisitions)¶

    Scope, methodology, and limitations¶

    This report is a long-form, desk-based synthesis of how cybersecurity due diligence is scoped, delivered, and priced in mergers and acquisitions. It consolidates common consulting approaches, typical deliverables, and directional rate ranges used to budget buy-side and sell-side diligence. The goal is to help corporate development teams, private equity firms, and security leaders align on scope, timing, and cost drivers.

    Important limitations apply: this document does not use live web browsing or real-time market data. Rate ranges are indicative planning ranges rather than verified quotes, and should be validated through competitive bids and legal review. Use the material as a structured starting point, then calibrate with current proposals, regional labor conditions, and industry-specific constraints.

    Note: This is not legal, financial, or insurance advice. It is a practical guide for scoping and budgeting cyber due diligence engagements.

    Executive summary¶

    • Cybersecurity due diligence is often compressed into 2–6 weeks, making scope clarity the most important driver of success.
    • Pricing is primarily driven by deal size, depth of testing, regulatory exposure, and speed-to-close rather than by a single universal rate.
    • Most engagements blend document review, management interviews, and targeted technical testing; deeper testing increases cost but reduces integration risk.
    • Common deliverables include a red-flag memo, a prioritized risk register, remediation cost estimates, and a 100-day integration plan.
    • Indicative hourly rates for M&A cyber diligence typically range from $150–$350 for independent consultants, $250–$600 for mid-tier firms, and $400–$900+ for global firms, with blended team rates often lower.
    • Sell-side diligence can reduce surprises and shorten the buyer’s diligence window, but independence and conflict-of-interest considerations matter.

    How to use this report¶

    • Use the scope and workstream sections to build or validate a diligence request list.
    • Use the depth tiers to align effort with deal timeline and risk tolerance.
    • Use the rate and budget sections as directional inputs for an RFP or internal budget.
    • Use the deliverables and red-flag sections to align on decision gates and escalation triggers.

    Market context and demand drivers¶

    • Regulatory exposure: sector-specific requirements (financial services, healthcare, critical infrastructure) increase scrutiny and diligence depth.
    • Cyber insurance pressure: insurers and lenders increasingly ask for diligence artifacts and control maturity evidence.
    • Cloud and SaaS sprawl: distributed architectures and third-party dependencies require more technical validation.
    • Incident visibility: breach history and vulnerability disclosure practices materially affect valuation and integration planning.
    • Investor expectations: private equity and strategic acquirers expect a quantified view of cyber risk in investment committee memos.

    Where cybersecurity due diligence fits in the M&A lifecycle¶

    Cyber due diligence runs in parallel with legal, financial, and operational diligence. It is commonly staged to match deal momentum.

    1. Target screening (pre-LOI): lightweight external attack-surface review and public signals.
    2. LOI to confirmatory diligence: data room review, interviews, and internal evidence analysis.
    3. Signing and close: finalize red flags, closing conditions, and remediation commitments.
    4. Integration planning: design the 30/60/90-day security integration roadmap.
    5. Post-close execution: deliver on remediation, tool integration, and risk reduction initiatives.

    Transaction types and risk allocation¶

    Asset purchase¶

    Risk is often limited to acquired assets, but legacy systems and shared services can be hidden. Cyber diligence focuses on the assets being carved out, transition service agreements, and data transfer risks.

    Stock purchase¶

    The buyer inherits historical liabilities. Diligence must include incident history, regulatory exposure, and security control maturity across the entire entity.

    Carve-out or divestiture¶

    Complexity increases due to shared infrastructure and interdependencies. Diligence must map what stays, what moves, and what is rebuilt.

    Minority investment or joint venture¶

    Governance and control rights determine how much visibility is achievable. Diligence often focuses on board-level risk oversight and contractual rights.

    Distressed or turnaround deals¶

    Security maturity may be low; risk tolerance must be explicit. Diligence typically emphasizes critical controls, ransomware exposure, and business continuity.

    Stakeholder map and decision rights¶

    • Corporate development / PE deal team: owns scope, timeline, and go/no-go decisions.
    • CISO / security lead: validates control maturity, interprets technical findings, and advises on remediation costs.
    • IT leadership: confirms system inventories, access dependencies, and integration feasibility.
    • Legal / compliance: aligns on regulatory exposure, privacy liabilities, and contract language.
    • Finance: translates remediation and insurance costs into valuation adjustments.
    • Risk management / insurance: evaluates coverage gaps and exclusions.
    • Integration management office: plans Day-1 and 100-day security integration initiatives.

    Core diligence workstreams¶

    Workstream Key Questions Typical Evidence
    Governance and program maturity Is there a formal security program with clear ownership and budget? Policies, org charts, risk registers, security budget summaries
    Asset inventory and CMDB Are systems, data flows, and cloud accounts fully inventoried? CMDB exports, cloud account lists, architecture diagrams
    Identity and access management Are privileged accounts controlled and monitored? IAM policies, MFA coverage reports, PAM configurations
    Network and endpoint security Is the environment segmented and monitored? Network diagrams, EDR coverage reports, firewall policies
    Cloud security posture Are cloud configurations aligned to best practices? CSPM reports, IAM role reviews, logging configurations
    Application security and SDLC Are applications tested and patched consistently? SDL policies, SAST/DAST results, code review evidence
    Data protection and privacy Is sensitive data classified and encrypted? Data classification, encryption settings, DLP policies
    Incident history and response Have there been material incidents or regulatory notifications? IR plans, incident logs, post-incident reports
    Third-party and supply-chain risk Are critical vendors assessed and monitored? Vendor risk assessments, contracts, SOC reports
    Compliance and regulatory Are obligations met and validated? SOC 2/ISO reports, PCI/HIPAA evidence, audit reports
    Business continuity and resilience Are backups and recovery tested? BCDR plans, RTO/RPO metrics, backup test logs
    Physical and OT (if applicable) Are facilities and OT environments secured? Facility access logs, OT network diagrams, safety controls

    Evidence sources and data room checklist¶

    Evidence review is typically anchored in a data room and targeted interviews. The list below covers common artifacts requested in buy-side diligence.

    • Security policies, standards, and control frameworks.
    • Organizational charts, security team roster, and budget summaries.
    • Asset inventory, network diagrams, and cloud account list.
    • IAM and privileged access policies, MFA coverage, and access review evidence.
    • Vulnerability management process, scan summaries, and remediation metrics.
    • Application security program documentation and code review cadence.
    • Incident response plan, tabletop exercises, incident history, and regulatory notifications.
    • Backup, recovery, and business continuity plans with test evidence.
    • Third-party risk assessments, key vendor contracts, and subprocessor lists.
    • Recent audit reports, certifications, and compliance artifacts.
    • Data classification scheme, encryption strategy, and privacy impact assessments.
    • Security awareness training materials and completion rates.
    • Bug bounty or external security testing reports.

    Diligence depth levels (tiers)¶

    Aligning the depth of diligence with the deal timeline reduces friction and ensures the right questions are asked.

    Tier Description Typical Duration Typical Effort Use Cases
    Tier 0 Rapid red-flag scan (external footprint + high-level review) 3–7 days 20–40 hours Early screening, small deals, time pressure
    Tier 1 Standard diligence (docs + interviews + limited technical review) 2–4 weeks 80–200 hours Most mid-market acquisitions
    Tier 2 Deep diligence (Tier 1 + targeted testing + architecture review) 4–8 weeks 200–500 hours Regulated industries, higher risk deals
    Tier 3 Full-scope diligence (Tier 2 + code review, segmentation validation, integration planning) 6–12 weeks 400–800+ hours Large or highly regulated transactions

    Technical testing options and safe bounds¶

    Technical testing is often the most sensitive part of due diligence. It must be explicitly authorized and coordinated to avoid operational disruption.

    • External attack-surface review: internet-facing assets, DNS, certificate hygiene, and exposed services.
    • Credentialed vulnerability scans: internal systems review with agreed safe windows.
    • Configuration reviews: firewall rules, cloud IAM, logging, and endpoint controls.
    • Cloud security posture analysis: CSPM results, permission boundaries, data exposure checks.
    • Application security review: high-level SDLC review or targeted code analysis for critical applications.
    • Penetration testing (scoped): highly controlled test of critical systems.
    • Red/purple team exercises: rare during diligence, typically reserved for post-close or pre-close with explicit approval.
    • Data protection validation: encryption, key management, and access controls for sensitive data.

    Deliverables and outputs¶

    Buy-side deliverables¶

    • Executive summary and red-flag memo for investment committee.
    • Prioritized risk register with severity, likelihood, and impact.
    • Remediation cost estimates and approximate timeline.
    • Closing-condition recommendations or indemnity suggestions.
    • 100-day security integration roadmap and tool alignment plan.

    Sell-side deliverables¶

    • Readiness assessment and gap remediation plan.
    • Evidence package optimized for buyer data room use.
    • Management Q&A playbook and clarification notes.
    • Pre-close mitigation timeline for identified weaknesses.

    Lender or insurer deliverables¶

    • Risk memo focused on material control gaps and exposure.
    • Summary of incident history and response maturity.
    • Insurance readiness notes and key coverage exclusions.

    Risk rating, materiality, and valuation impact¶

    Cyber diligence findings matter most when they translate into financial or operational exposure. Effective reporting classifies issues by materiality and recommends a corresponding deal action.

    Typical risk tiers and actions: - Critical (deal breaker or closing condition): unresolved breach, systemic access control failure, or regulatory noncompliance with imminent enforcement. - High (valuation or indemnity impact): significant control gaps, weak vulnerability management, or missing incident response capability. - Medium (integration plan impact): patching delays, limited monitoring coverage, or incomplete policies. - Low (operational hygiene): documentation inconsistencies or minor tooling gaps.

    Common valuation levers: - Purchase price adjustments or escrow holdbacks. - Specific indemnities or warranties related to cyber incidents. - Pre-close remediation commitments with evidence milestones. - Insurance premium adjustments or exclusions. - Integration budget increases for remediation and tooling.

    Pricing structures and rate drivers¶

    Common pricing models¶

    • Time and materials (T&M): most common for uncertain scope.
    • Fixed fee: suitable when deliverables and timelines are well-defined.
    • Hybrid: fixed fee for baseline diligence with T&M for out-of-scope testing.
    • Retainer or advisory: used for ongoing pre-LOI screening across multiple targets.

    Key rate drivers¶

    • Deal size and risk tolerance: higher valuations often demand deeper diligence.
    • Industry regulation: healthcare, finance, and critical infrastructure demand more evidence.
    • Data sensitivity: PII, PHI, payment data, or IP-heavy products increase scope.
    • Access and data room quality: limited access increases effort and uncertainty.
    • Historical incidents: breach history adds investigative work and legal coordination.
    • Infrastructure complexity: multi-cloud, legacy systems, or global footprints add effort.
    • Timeline urgency: accelerated diligence commands premiums.
    • Testing depth: code review or penetration testing increases cost.

    Indicative rate ranges (USD)¶

    The ranges below are directional planning ranges for budgeting. Validate with local market quotes and vendor proposals.

    Hourly rate ranges by provider type¶

    Provider Type Typical Range Notes
    Independent consultants $150–$350/hr Senior specialists may exceed this range
    Boutique security firms $200–$450/hr Often blend lead + analyst for efficiency
    Mid-tier consultancies $250–$600/hr Higher rates for partner-led work
    Large global firms $350–$900+/hr Premium for brand, scale, and speed
    Specialized testing teams $200–$500+/hr Applies to pen test or code review specialists
    Offshore/nearshore analysts $60–$150/hr Typically used for evidence review support

    Role ladder and typical rate bands¶

    Role Typical Experience Typical Range
    Analyst / Associate 2–5 years $80–$160/hr
    Consultant / Senior 5–9 years $150–$300/hr
    Manager / Lead 8–12 years $220–$400/hr
    Principal / Partner 12+ years $300–$700+/hr

    Day, week, and month conversions (approx.)¶

    • Day rate (8 hours): $640–$5,600+
    • Week rate (40 hours): $3,200–$28,000+
    • Monthly retainer (20–40 hours): $3,000–$25,000+ depending on rate band

    Specialized testing adders (directional)¶

    • Penetration test add-on: $10k–$60k depending on scope and targets.
    • Targeted code review: $15k–$80k depending on codebase size and access.
    • Cloud posture review add-on: $10k–$50k depending on number of accounts.

    Regional rate multipliers (relative to US baseline)¶

    • North America: 1.0x baseline
    • Western Europe: 0.8–1.0x
    • Eastern Europe: 0.5–0.8x
    • LATAM: 0.4–0.7x
    • India / South Asia: 0.3–0.6x
    • Australia / NZ: 0.9–1.1x
    • Middle East (specialist): 0.9–1.2x

    Typical project budget ranges (indicative)¶

    These ranges describe consulting-only costs and exclude external audit fees or long-term remediation.

    By deal size¶

    • Under $50M enterprise value: $15k–$60k (Tier 0–1), $60k–$150k (Tier 2).
    • $50M–$250M: $40k–$150k (Tier 1), $150k–$350k (Tier 2).
    • $250M–$1B: $150k–$400k (Tier 1–2), $400k–$900k (Tier 3).
    • $1B+: $400k–$1.5M+ depending on footprint and regulatory scope.

    By diligence depth¶

    • Rapid screen (Tier 0): $5k–$30k.
    • Standard diligence (Tier 1): $25k–$150k.
    • Deep diligence (Tier 2): $100k–$500k.
    • Full-scope diligence (Tier 3): $250k–$1M+.

    By industry complexity (adjustments)¶

    • SaaS / B2B software: baseline ranges above.
    • Fintech / payments / banking: add 20–60% for regulatory review.
    • Healthcare / life sciences: add 20–70% for PHI and compliance checks.
    • Critical infrastructure / OT: add 30–100% for site visits and OT controls.

    Sell-side vs buy-side pricing patterns¶

    • Sell-side diligence is often lower-cost and shorter, focused on preparing an evidence package and addressing obvious gaps.
    • Buy-side diligence is typically deeper and more skeptical, driving additional testing and independent validation.
    • Some transactions use a sell-side assessment as a baseline, then add buyer-specific testing.

    Special cases that drive scope increases¶

    • Carve-outs and shared services: require detailed mapping of shared identity, networks, and data flows.
    • Cross-border transactions: add complexity for privacy, data residency, and regulatory requirements.
    • High incident history: requires forensic review and legal coordination.
    • Legacy systems: increase remediation effort and integration risk.
    • Aggressive timelines: increase staffing and drive premium pricing.

    Timeline and resourcing assumptions¶

    Below is a representative 4–6 week timeline for a Tier 1–2 diligence engagement.

    • Week 1: kickoff, data room access, initial questionnaires.
    • Week 2: document review, stakeholder interviews, early red flags.
    • Week 3: targeted technical testing, validation of controls.
    • Week 4: risk scoring, remediation estimation, draft report.
    • Week 5–6 (if needed): integration planning, management Q&A, final report.

    Scoping questionnaire (buyer-side)¶

    Use these questions to align stakeholders on scope and expectations.

    • What is the deal type (asset, stock, carve-out) and expected close date?
    • What is the target’s revenue, employee count, and data sensitivity profile?
    • What are the critical products and systems that drive valuation?
    • Which regulatory regimes apply (PCI, HIPAA, GDPR, SOX, etc.)?
    • What level of technical testing is authorized and feasible?
    • What is the desired depth tier (0–3) and acceptable cost range?
    • What are the buyer’s integration priorities and tool stack standards?
    • Are there known incidents, regulatory actions, or ongoing investigations?
    • What is the deal team’s tolerance for remediation commitments?

    Data request list (sample)¶

    Organize requests by workstream to minimize back-and-forth.

    Governance and organization¶

    • Security strategy, governance charters, and organizational chart.
    • Security budget summaries and staffing levels.
    • Risk assessments and board-level reporting.

    Asset inventory and architecture¶

    • Network diagrams and data flow maps.
    • Cloud account inventory and key services list.
    • CMDB exports and asset classification.

    Identity and access¶

    • IAM policies and MFA coverage metrics.
    • Privileged access management documentation.
    • Access review evidence (last 12 months).

    Vulnerability and patching¶

    • Vulnerability scan summaries and remediation SLA metrics.
    • Patch management process and exception reports.
    • External attack-surface monitoring outputs (if available).

    Application security¶

    • SDLC policy, code review standards, and pipeline security controls.
    • SAST/DAST results and secure coding training records.
    • Third-party dependency management practices.

    Data protection and privacy¶

    • Data classification policy and encryption standards.
    • DLP controls, key management practices, and privacy impact assessments.
    • Data retention and deletion policies.

    Incident response and resilience¶

    • IR plan, playbooks, and tabletop exercise summaries.
    • Incident history for the last 24–36 months.
    • Backup and recovery test evidence.

    Third-party risk¶

    • Critical vendor list and due diligence outcomes.
    • Vendor contracts with security clauses.
    • SOC reports or equivalent assurance for key vendors.

    Compliance evidence¶

    • SOC 2 or ISO 27001 reports (if applicable).
    • PCI/HIPAA/GxP evidence and audit results.
    • Regulatory correspondence or enforcement notices.

    RFP / SOW outline¶

    • Engagement objectives and deal timeline.
    • Required diligence depth tier and testing permissions.
    • Workstreams and deliverables list.
    • Data room access and confidentiality requirements.
    • Reporting format and decision gates.
    • Pricing model, assumptions, and change control.
    • Staff qualifications and conflict-of-interest disclosures.

    Integration and 100-day security plan¶

    Post-close integration is where diligence findings translate into action. A good plan links remediation to business priorities.

    • Establish security governance and risk ownership for the combined entity.
    • Prioritize remediation for critical risks identified during diligence.
    • Align identity, logging, and endpoint tooling across environments.
    • Harden cloud access policies and consolidate key management.
    • Update incident response plans for the combined environment.
    • Confirm compliance requirements for new data flows and shared services.

    Cyber insurance and reps & warranties considerations¶

    • Diligence findings often inform representations, warranties, and indemnity carve-outs.
    • Known incidents or control gaps may drive insurance exclusions or premium increases.
    • Ensure diligence artifacts are retained for underwriting or claim support.

    Red flags and escalation triggers¶

    • Unresolved security incidents or breach disclosures.
    • No formal incident response capability or tested backups.
    • Lack of MFA for privileged or remote access.
    • No vulnerability management or patching cadence.
    • Incomplete asset inventory or cloud account visibility.
    • Regulatory investigation or noncompliance exposure.

    Vendor selection criteria¶

    • Demonstrated M&A diligence experience and deal references.
    • Ability to scale a blended team with clear senior oversight.
    • Neutrality and absence of product sales incentives.
    • Clear testing methodology with safety controls.
    • Ability to translate technical findings into deal terms.

    Budget estimation model (quick calculator)¶

    Use this approach to sanity-check quotes.

    1. Select a base tier: Tier 0 (20–40 hrs), Tier 1 (80–200 hrs), Tier 2 (200–500 hrs).
    2. Add complexity factors: multi-cloud (+20–40%), regulated industry (+20–60%), global footprint (+15–40%).
    3. Add testing components: pen test or code review (+50–150 hrs each).
    4. Apply blended rate: weighted average of lead and analyst rates.
    5. Add contingency: 10–20% for scope shifts or data delays.

    Example formula:

    Estimated hours = Base tier hours * (1 + complexity modifiers) + testing hours
Project cost = Estimated hours * blended rate + fixed testing add-ons

    Example scenarios (illustrative)¶

    These scenarios are illustrative only and should be validated with live quotes.

    Scenario 1: SaaS acquisition, $40M EV, Tier 1¶

    • Scope: document review, interviews, limited cloud posture review.
    • Effort: 100–160 hours.
    • Budget range: $25k–$60k.

    Scenario 2: Fintech acquisition, $200M EV, Tier 2¶

    • Scope: Tier 1 plus targeted penetration test and compliance review.
    • Effort: 250–450 hours.
    • Budget range: $120k–$300k.

    Scenario 3: Industrial carve-out, $600M EV, Tier 2–3¶

    • Scope: OT/IT segmentation review, site visits, and integration roadmap.
    • Effort: 400–700 hours.
    • Budget range: $250k–$700k.

    Scenario 4: Global healthcare target, $1.2B EV, Tier 3¶

    • Scope: deep technical testing, privacy compliance review, and 100-day plan.
    • Effort: 700–1,000+ hours.
    • Budget range: $600k–$1.5M+.

    Common pitfalls and mitigations¶

    • Unclear testing permissions: resolve scope and authorization during kickoff.
    • Data room delays: require a minimum evidence package before starting.
    • Over-scoped requests: align deliverables with investment committee needs.
    • Lack of remediation ownership: assign owners and timelines early.
    • No integration plan: convert findings into Day-1 and 100-day actions.

    Appendix: Communication cadence and reporting¶

    • Weekly status updates with red-flag tracking.
    • Midpoint readout to validate emerging risks.
    • Final report with executive summary, risk register, and remediation plan.

    Appendix: Due diligence report outline¶

    1. Executive summary and red flags.
    2. Deal context and scope assumptions.
    3. Diligence methodology and data sources.
    4. Findings by workstream with severity ratings.
    5. Remediation cost estimates and timeline.
    6. Integration roadmap and governance recommendations.
    7. Appendices with evidence inventory and interview list.

    Closing notes¶

    Cybersecurity due diligence is most valuable when it produces clear, decision-ready outputs rather than exhaustive technical detail. Use the scope and rate guidance here to build a proportional diligence plan that aligns with the deal thesis, regulatory exposure, and integration strategy. Validate rates with market quotes and tailor depth to the materiality of the target’s data and operations.
