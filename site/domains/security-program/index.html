    1. Executive Summary¶

    Security program development consulting focuses on building a structured, measurable, and sustainable security capability that aligns with business strategy, regulatory requirements, and risk appetite. Most organizations have ad-hoc controls, isolated tooling, and incomplete policies; consultants help translate that fragmented state into a coherent program with governance, control maturity, and clear accountability. A typical engagement includes discovery, maturity assessment, target state definition, gap analysis, and a prioritized roadmap. Delivery often extends into policy development, security architecture guidance, operating model design, and support for tooling selection and implementation.

    Roadmap consulting emphasizes sequencing and dependency management rather than just listing tasks. Effective roadmaps balance foundational governance (risk management, policies, asset inventory) with technical controls (identity, monitoring, vulnerability management) and human factors (training, accountability). Phased plans prioritize rapid risk reduction and compliance readiness while avoiding “big bang” implementations that overwhelm internal teams. Because security is cross-functional, roadmap work must integrate with engineering, IT, legal, HR, procurement, and executive sponsors.

    Program maturity assessments sit at the front of this workstream. They benchmark current capabilities against frameworks such as NIST CSF, ISO 27001, CIS Controls, or industry-specific requirements. Maturity scores provide a fact base to justify investment, inform audit planning, and align expectations with leadership. Assessments range from lightweight (2–4 weeks, document review and interviews) to comprehensive (8–12 weeks, evidence validation and technical testing). Rates vary widely by region, firm size, and service depth; in mature markets, typical consulting rates range from $150–$350/hour for senior consultants and $250–$600/hour for principals or specialized SMEs, with boutique firms sometimes offering lower hourly rates and more flexible packages.

    This report provides a pragmatic view of how consulting is structured, the outcomes to expect, and a detailed breakdown of maturity assessment and roadmap consulting costs. It includes readiness checklists, deliverable templates, and rate benchmarks to help clients plan budgets and evaluate proposals. While prices are presented as ranges, the most important determinants are scope clarity, organizational complexity, regulatory pressure, data sensitivity, and the pace of change required by leadership.

    2. Scope and Methodology¶

    This research synthesizes common consulting practices across enterprise security, governance risk and compliance (GRC), and security architecture advisory services. It focuses on two core offerings: (1) security program development, including roadmap creation and foundational capability building, and (2) maturity assessment services, including rate benchmarks and typical scope elements. The intent is to be broad enough for startups through large enterprises while still providing practical numbers and templates.

    Methodology is qualitative and experience-based. The report uses typical industry ranges, commonly observed delivery patterns, and standard framework mappings. It assumes a consulting context where the client has some internal IT capability but needs structured program management, risk clarity, and architectural guidance. It also assumes that organizations are seeking to meet baseline expectations for governance, regulatory compliance, and modern security practices, rather than pursuing niche or extremely specialized use cases (for example, industrial control systems or defense-grade requirements).

    3. Security Program Development Consulting Overview¶

    Security program development consulting is the practice of defining, building, and institutionalizing an organization’s security capability. The work goes beyond tool deployment and includes the operating model, governance, and accountability structures that make security sustainable. It typically combines strategy (roadmaps and target states), execution planning (projects, staffing, dependencies), and enabling artifacts (policies, standards, procedures, and metrics). The goal is not just compliance, but predictable risk management aligned with business objectives.

    Consulting engagements often begin with a maturity assessment or discovery effort to understand the existing state. This includes interviews, policy reviews, architecture mapping, control testing, and review of incident history. Consultants then translate findings into a prioritized roadmap, often with a cost model, staffing plan, and sequence of initiatives. Many projects include quick wins such as multi-factor authentication, vulnerability scanning, or incident response runbooks to reduce risk while longer-term projects (like data governance or zero trust architecture) are designed.

    The consulting value proposition includes unbiased assessment, structured methodologies, access to specialized subject matter expertise, and the ability to mobilize quickly. For organizations with limited security leadership, consultants can act as interim or fractional CISO roles, providing governance while internal teams ramp. For larger organizations, consultants augment specialist areas such as cloud security, governance, privacy, or regulatory readiness.

    4. Market Drivers and Demand Signals¶

    Demand for security program consulting is driven by increasing regulatory scrutiny, the proliferation of data protection requirements, and operational risk from ransomware and supply-chain attacks. Boards and executive leaders are expected to demonstrate oversight, which requires formalized programs with clear documentation. As a result, organizations are investing in maturity assessments and roadmaps to justify budget allocation and show progress to stakeholders.

    Another major demand signal is digital transformation. Cloud migration, remote work, SaaS adoption, and API-driven ecosystems expand the attack surface and complicate control ownership. Security programs must evolve quickly, and organizations often lack the internal bandwidth or expertise to redesign governance, identity, and monitoring programs alone. Consulting provides structured acceleration during these transition periods.

    Growth-stage companies also face external pressure from investors, enterprise customers, and cyber insurance requirements. Many security programs are built to meet SOC 2, ISO 27001, or other certifications that unlock sales. Consulting engagements in these contexts emphasize policy development, control implementation planning, and audit readiness, often within aggressive timelines.

    5. Engagement Models and Staffing Structures¶

    Security program consulting engagements typically fall into one of four models: assessment, roadmap development, implementation advisory, and managed or retainer-based guidance. Each model can be delivered by a boutique firm, a large consulting organization, or independent specialists. The model chosen affects price and outcomes.

    Common engagement models

    • Maturity assessment only: Short engagement focused on baseline maturity and gaps. Output includes scoring and prioritized recommendations.
    • Assessment + roadmap: Adds multi-year planning, sequencing, and resource modeling.
    • Program build: Includes policy development, operating model design, and direct support to implement controls.
    • Fractional CISO / retainer: Ongoing strategic guidance and governance for organizations without full-time leadership.

    Staffing often includes a mix of roles: a lead consultant or principal, a project manager, domain SMEs (identity, cloud, GRC), and analysts to perform evidence collection. This blended model keeps costs reasonable while ensuring specialized depth where necessary. In small engagements, one or two senior consultants may handle most work, while larger enterprises require multi-stream teams with dedicated workstream leads.

    6. Security Program Lifecycle and Roadmap Consulting¶

    Security roadmaps translate strategy into actionable initiatives. A good roadmap is not just a list of projects; it is a staged plan that accounts for dependencies, organizational readiness, and operational capacity. Roadmap consulting typically proceeds through phases:

    1. Discovery and current-state analysis: Gather artifacts, map systems and data flows, interview stakeholders, and assess existing controls.
    2. Target-state definition: Agree on desired outcomes, maturity targets, regulatory requirements, and business constraints.
    3. Gap analysis: Compare current state against target state using framework mappings and risk analysis.
    4. Prioritization and sequencing: Identify quick wins, dependencies, and foundational prerequisites.
    5. Resource and cost modeling: Estimate effort, staffing, and budget for each roadmap initiative.
    6. Governance setup: Define decision rights, reporting cadence, and success metrics.

    Roadmap consultants typically recommend a three-horizon structure:

    • 0–3 months (stabilize): Address immediate risks and visibility gaps (MFA, backups, endpoint coverage, vulnerability scanning).
    • 3–12 months (build): Develop policies, governance, and standard control processes (risk management, incident response, access reviews).
    • 12–24 months (optimize): Mature analytics, automation, zero trust architecture, and business integration.

    The roadmap should explicitly align with business milestones, such as product launches, compliance audits, or acquisitions. It should also account for organizational change management, since policy enforcement and new tooling can create friction if adoption is not planned.

    7. Core Program Domains and Capabilities¶

    Security programs are typically built across a set of core domains. Each domain has associated policies, controls, and metrics, and most maturity assessments evaluate these domains separately. The list below is a common consolidation used in consulting engagements.

    Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC)¶

    The GRC domain establishes how security decisions are made, how risks are identified and tracked, and how compliance obligations are managed. Core elements include risk registers, policy management, compliance mapping, and third-party risk processes. A mature GRC program ensures that security activities are prioritized based on risk impact and that leadership receives regular reporting on progress and gaps.

    Asset and Configuration Management¶

    Effective security requires knowing what is owned and where it resides. Asset management includes inventories of devices, applications, cloud resources, and data stores. Configuration management ensures secure baselines and standardized builds. Many breaches stem from unmanaged assets or misconfigurations, so this domain is frequently a high-priority area for roadmap work.

    Identity and Access Management (IAM)¶

    IAM is foundational because it governs who can access what systems and data. Core capabilities include MFA, single sign-on, privileged access management, access provisioning/deprovisioning, and access reviews. Mature programs integrate IAM with HR and IT workflows to reduce orphaned accounts and improve auditability.

    Data Protection and Privacy¶

    This domain covers data classification, encryption, retention policies, data loss prevention (DLP), and privacy obligations. It intersects with legal and compliance teams, especially when handling regulated data (PII, PHI, PCI). Roadmaps often focus on data discovery, classification, and encryption as early steps.

    Security Architecture and Engineering¶

    Security architecture defines patterns and guardrails for system design. It includes secure network segmentation, zero trust strategies, secure software development practices, and approval processes for high-risk systems. Consulting often involves developing standards and reference architectures, then integrating them into engineering workflows.

    Vulnerability and Patch Management¶

    Vulnerability management is a continuous process of scanning, prioritizing, and remediating weaknesses. Mature programs use risk-based prioritization, integrate with ticketing systems, and track remediation SLAs. For many organizations, this domain provides rapid risk reduction when standardized.

    Security Monitoring and Detection¶

    Detection capabilities include logging, SIEM, alert triage, and incident escalation. Organizations with limited monitoring often prioritize logging and alerting coverage before moving into advanced analytics and automation. Consulting can help set log retention standards and alerting thresholds aligned with business risk.

    Incident Response and Crisis Management¶

    Incident response includes playbooks, escalation paths, communications, and post-incident reviews. It is essential for resilience and regulatory compliance. Roadmaps typically recommend creating or refining response plans, testing them through tabletop exercises, and integrating with business continuity plans.

    Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery (BCDR)¶

    BCDR addresses resilience of critical business services. It includes recovery time objectives, backup strategies, failover testing, and disaster recovery planning. This domain is often coordinated with IT operations but must align with security objectives to ensure that backups are protected and tested.

    Third-Party and Supply Chain Risk¶

    Third-party risk management evaluates the security posture of vendors and partners. It includes vendor questionnaires, contract clauses, monitoring of critical suppliers, and exit plans. Consulting engagements frequently help design scalable TPRM processes that balance risk and procurement speed.

    Security Awareness and Culture¶

    Human behavior is a significant risk factor. Awareness programs cover training, phishing simulations, role-based education, and executive involvement. Mature programs measure participation and track behavioral changes over time.

    8. Maturity Assessment Models and Definitions¶

    Maturity models provide a structured lens for evaluating security capabilities. Common models used in consulting include the NIST CSF tiers, CMMI-style capability levels, and ISO 27001 control maturity concepts. While frameworks differ, a typical maturity scale has five levels:

    1. Initial / Ad-hoc: Controls are unstructured, reactive, and inconsistent.
    2. Repeatable / Emerging: Some standard processes exist, but they are not consistently enforced.
    3. Defined: Policies and processes are documented and largely followed.
    4. Managed / Measured: Controls are monitored with metrics, and performance is regularly reviewed.
    5. Optimized: Controls are continuously improved with automation and advanced analytics.

    In consulting practice, maturity models are tailored to the organization’s industry, regulatory needs, and risk tolerance. For example, a startup may target Level 2–3 for most domains, while a regulated enterprise may need Level 4 in identity, data protection, and incident response.

    9. Maturity Assessment Methodology¶

    Maturity assessments are structured exercises to establish baseline capability and identify gaps. Most methodologies include the following steps:

    1. Planning and scoping: Identify business units, systems, and regulatory drivers to include.
    2. Stakeholder interviews: Gather perspectives from IT, security, legal, operations, and leadership.
    3. Artifact collection: Review policies, procedures, logs, and evidence of control operation.
    4. Technical validation: Spot-check system configurations, access controls, and monitoring coverage.
    5. Scoring and analysis: Rate each domain across maturity levels.
    6. Report and roadmap: Present findings, risk themes, and prioritized recommendations.

    Assessments vary in depth. A rapid assessment may rely heavily on interviews and documents, while a comprehensive assessment includes configuration reviews, control testing, and multiple stakeholder workshops. The output should be defensible and repeatable, enabling year-over-year comparisons.

    10. Scoring, Normalization, and Reporting¶

    Scoring typically combines qualitative judgments with evidence-based scoring. Many consultants use a 1–5 scale and map evidence to control statements. For example, the control “MFA enforced for privileged access” might be scored as 1 if absent, 3 if partially deployed, and 5 if fully enforced with monitoring and exception handling. Scores may be averaged by domain or weighted by risk impact.

    Normalization is important when comparing across domains. Some controls naturally lag due to dependencies or organizational size, and a raw average can misrepresent priorities. A common approach is to categorize controls as foundational, advanced, or strategic, then focus on foundational gaps regardless of score averages. Another approach is to compute a weighted maturity index based on critical assets or regulatory obligations.

    Reporting should focus on decision-making. The best reports include: a maturity heatmap, top risk themes, quick wins, and a roadmap with sequencing and budget implications. Visuals such as radar charts, heatmaps, and progress trackers help executives understand progress without getting lost in technical details.

    12. Pricing Fundamentals and Rate Drivers¶

    Security consulting rates are influenced by multiple variables, including region, firm size, consultant seniority, regulatory complexity, and scope uncertainty. Rates also reflect the scarcity of specialized skills, such as cloud security architecture, incident response, or compliance expertise in highly regulated sectors.

    Key rate drivers include:

    • Scope complexity: Multi-site, global operations, or highly regulated data add cost.
    • Depth of assessment: Evidence validation and technical testing require more time and higher skill.
    • Industry requirements: Healthcare, finance, and critical infrastructure often demand more documentation and specialized knowledge.
    • Delivery urgency: Accelerated timelines drive premium rates or higher staffing.
    • Consultant reputation: Well-known firms charge premiums for brand trust and established methodologies.
    • Travel and onsite requirements: Onsite work increases costs and may reduce flexibility.

    Procurement teams should calibrate pricing based on deliverables and level of assurance required. A low-cost assessment may be sufficient for internal planning, while audit-driven or regulatory contexts often need deeper validation and higher-cost engagement models.

    18. Budgeting Guidance by Company Stage¶

    Security program budgets depend on revenue, risk exposure, regulatory obligations, and the pace of change. Below are simplified guidelines for planning.

    Startup / Early-Stage (50–200 employees)¶

    • Security program consulting budget: $30k–$150k annually
    • Primary focus: baseline policies, SOC 2 readiness, IAM, incident response
    • Typical tools: managed endpoint protection, cloud logging, simple GRC tooling

    Growth / Mid-Market (200–1,500 employees)¶

    • Consulting budget: $150k–$600k annually
    • Focus: formal risk management, vulnerability management, data classification, TPRM
    • Likely needs: dedicated security leader, expanded monitoring, audit readiness

    Enterprise (1,500+ employees)¶

    • Consulting budget: $600k–$3M annually
    • Focus: multi-region governance, zero trust architecture, privacy operations
    • Likely needs: multi-stream program office, tooling integration, specialized SMEs

    Budget allocation often follows a people-process-technology split. Early-stage companies may spend 40–50% on consulting and process development, while enterprises allocate more to technology and specialized internal teams.

    19. Deliverables and Artifacts Checklist¶

    A complete security program development engagement typically yields a mix of strategic and operational deliverables. Common artifacts include:

    • Current-state assessment report with maturity scores
    • Risk register with prioritized risk themes
    • Target-state architecture and control objectives
    • 12–24 month roadmap with dependencies and sequencing
    • Policy framework and key policies (security, access, data, incident response)
    • Control catalog mapped to relevant frameworks
    • Operating model and RACI matrix
    • Metrics dashboard and reporting cadence
    • Budget and staffing recommendations
    • Tooling recommendations and integration plan

    The number of deliverables should scale with project scope. A small engagement may provide a subset (assessment, roadmap, key policies), while larger programs include operational procedures and training content.

    20. Metrics, KPIs, and Success Criteria¶

    Security program metrics should be simple, measurable, and directly tied to risk reduction or compliance outcomes. Common KPIs include:

    • Percentage of systems with MFA enforced
    • Vulnerability remediation within SLA (e.g., critical within 7 days)
    • Coverage of logging and monitoring for critical assets
    • Percentage of employees completing security training
    • Number of third-party vendors assessed before onboarding
    • Mean time to detect and respond to incidents
    • Audit findings closed on time

    Success criteria should be tied to business outcomes, such as reduced downtime, fewer audit findings, or improved customer trust. Consulting engagements should define these metrics at the outset to avoid subjective assessments of progress.

    21. Sample 12–24 Month Roadmap¶

    Below is a high-level example of a roadmap structure. Actual sequencing depends on business priorities and resource constraints.

    Quarter 1–2 (Foundation)

    • Establish governance committee and risk reporting cadence
    • Implement MFA for all privileged accounts
    • Launch asset inventory and baseline vulnerability scanning
    • Draft and approve core security policies

    Quarter 3–4 (Build)

    • Implement IAM access reviews and provisioning workflows
    • Stand up centralized logging and SIEM for critical systems
    • Develop incident response playbooks and run tabletop exercises
    • Formalize third-party risk management process

    Year 2 (Optimize)

    • Expand monitoring to cloud-native and application logs
    • Implement data classification and encryption standards
    • Mature security metrics and KPI dashboards
    • Introduce automation for vulnerability remediation and access management

    This roadmap assumes a mid-market organization with moderate maturity. Larger enterprises may extend each phase and introduce more parallel workstreams.

    22. Sample 90-Day Assessment Plan¶

    Weeks 1–2: Planning and kickoff

    • Confirm scope, stakeholders, and desired frameworks
    • Collect initial documentation and asset inventories
    • Schedule interviews and technical validation sessions

    Weeks 3–6: Evidence collection and interviews

    • Conduct stakeholder interviews across IT, security, legal, HR
    • Review policies, procedures, and system configurations
    • Collect sample evidence for key controls

    Weeks 7–8: Analysis and scoring

    • Score maturity across domains
    • Identify critical gaps and risk themes
    • Validate findings with stakeholders

    Weeks 9–10: Reporting and roadmap draft

    • Produce assessment report and maturity heatmap
    • Draft prioritized roadmap initiatives
    • Prepare executive summary and board-ready briefing

    Weeks 11–12: Finalization and handoff

    • Present findings to leadership
    • Deliver roadmap, budget estimate, and recommended next steps

    23. Procurement, SOW, and RFP Guidance¶

    When sourcing security program consulting, procurement teams should evaluate vendors on methodology, expertise, and fit with internal culture. A strong SOW should clearly describe scope, deliverables, assumptions, and success criteria.

    RFP evaluation criteria

    • Demonstrated experience in similar industries
    • Ability to map to relevant frameworks (NIST, ISO, SOC 2)
    • Specific deliverables and reporting formats
    • Team composition and level of senior involvement
    • References or case studies (if available)
    • Approach to change management and stakeholder alignment

    SOW best practices

    • Define scope boundaries (systems, business units, geographies)
    • Specify artifact expectations (policies, roadmaps, dashboards)
    • Include an engagement timeline with milestones
    • Clarify assumptions for client participation and data access
    • Outline post-assessment support or implementation advisory options

    24. Common Pitfalls and Success Factors¶

    Pitfalls

    • Over-scoping the assessment and delaying delivery
    • Focusing on tools before governance is defined
    • Ignoring change management and communication needs
    • Underestimating data classification and asset inventory effort
    • Treating maturity scoring as a goal rather than a diagnostic

    Success factors

    • Executive sponsorship and clear decision rights
    • Cross-functional involvement from IT, legal, HR, and product
    • Quick wins that demonstrate value early
    • A pragmatic roadmap with measurable milestones
    • Regular reporting to maintain accountability

    25. Integration with Engineering, Cloud, and Product¶

    Modern security programs must integrate with engineering and product teams rather than operate as standalone functions. This involves embedding security into software development life cycles (SDLC), establishing secure coding practices, and aligning security requirements with product delivery timelines. Consulting engagements that ignore product realities often produce roadmaps that are difficult to implement.

    For cloud environments, consultants should assess identity boundaries, logging coverage, network segmentation, and configuration drift. Cloud-native security tools can provide automation and visibility, but only if operating model changes are made to support them. Roadmaps should explicitly reference cloud landing zones, infrastructure-as-code guardrails, and security in CI/CD pipelines.

    Product security is a growing concern as SaaS businesses face increasing customer scrutiny. Consulting engagements may include secure development training, threat modeling, and product release criteria. These activities connect to broader program governance by ensuring that security requirements are built into business planning and delivery cycles.

    26. Privacy, Data Governance, and GRC Alignment¶

    Security roadmaps should align with privacy and data governance initiatives. Data classification and retention policies often fall under legal or privacy teams but require security controls to be effective. Consulting engagements can bridge this gap by mapping security controls to privacy obligations (e.g., encryption, access logging, data minimization).

    Many organizations adopt GRC platforms to manage policies, risks, and compliance tasks. Consultants should evaluate whether such platforms are mature enough to provide real-time visibility or whether simpler tools (spreadsheets and ticketing integrations) are sufficient in the near term. The goal is to avoid tool sprawl while enabling transparency.

    27. Third-Party, Incident Response, and BCDR¶

    Third-party risk management is often a pain point for growing organizations. Consultants typically establish a tiered vendor risk process, where critical vendors undergo deeper assessments and standard vendors follow lighter due diligence. This balance reduces procurement delays while still addressing risk. Key deliverables include vendor questionnaires, contract language, and monitoring procedures.

    Incident response and business continuity planning are closely connected. Effective response plans include clear escalation paths, communication templates, and technical playbooks. BCDR focuses on availability and recovery, with defined recovery time and recovery point objectives. Consultants often recommend joint exercises that test both incident response and recovery readiness to ensure alignment between security and operations.

    28. Training and Culture Development¶

    Security training is effective when it is role-specific and integrated into daily workflows. For example, engineers may need secure coding guidance, while executives need incident response decision training. Consulting engagements can help design a training plan, choose platforms, and set up metrics such as completion rates and phishing simulation results.

    Culture development extends beyond formal training. It includes communication from leadership, recognition programs, and consistent reinforcement of security expectations. Roadmaps should allocate time and budget for these cultural initiatives, especially during organizational change.

    29. ROI Narrative and Business Case Framing¶

    The ROI of security programs is often framed in terms of risk reduction, compliance enablement, and operational resilience. Consulting engagements should help leadership articulate how security investments support revenue growth (e.g., enabling enterprise sales), reduce downtime, and prevent costly incidents. While it is difficult to quantify avoided loss, risk-based scenarios can help justify funding.

    Another value driver is operational efficiency. Mature security programs reduce rework and firefighting by standardizing processes and enabling automation. This can lower long-term costs even if initial investment is significant. A roadmap with clear milestones and KPIs helps demonstrate progress and maintain executive support.

    Appendix A: Sample Maturity Assessment Questionnaire (Condensed)¶

    The following questions are organized by domain and can be used to structure interviews or evidence collection. Expand or reduce based on scope.

    Governance and Risk

    • Is there a documented security policy approved by leadership?
    • How frequently are security risks reviewed and updated?
    • Is there a formal risk register with owners and dates?
    • Are risk acceptance decisions documented with business justification?
    • Is security risk included in enterprise risk management reporting?
    • Are security roles and responsibilities documented and communicated?
    • Is there a security steering committee or governance forum?
    • How are security budgets planned and tracked?
    • Are security exceptions documented and reviewed periodically?
    • Is there a process for policy updates and version control?
    • Are compliance obligations mapped to control owners?
    • How are audit findings tracked to closure?

    Asset and Configuration Management

    • Is there a complete inventory of hardware and virtual assets?
    • Are cloud resources automatically discovered and tagged?
    • Are asset owners identified for critical systems?
    • Are secure configuration baselines defined for servers and endpoints?
    • Is configuration drift monitored and remediated?
    • Are asset inventories reconciled with procurement records?
    • Are unsupported or end-of-life systems tracked and remediated?
    • Are critical assets classified and prioritized for protection?
    • Is there a process for decommissioning assets securely?
    • Are CMDB entries validated for accuracy on a regular basis?
    • Are remote and mobile assets included in inventories?
    • Is there a centralized system to track software licenses?

    Identity and Access Management

    • Is MFA enforced for all privileged accounts?
    • Is SSO used for the majority of business applications?
    • Are access requests documented and approved?
    • Are access rights reviewed periodically for high-risk systems?
    • Is privileged access managed through dedicated tooling?
    • Are service accounts inventoried and monitored?
    • Is access deprovisioning automated upon employee departure?
    • Are shared accounts prohibited or controlled by policy?
    • Are admin actions logged and reviewed for anomalies?
    • Are role-based access controls defined for critical systems?
    • Is access to production systems restricted and monitored?
    • Are password policies enforced and aligned with best practices?

    Data Protection and Privacy

    • Is data classified by sensitivity and regulatory requirements?
    • Are encryption standards defined for data at rest and in transit?
    • Is there a data retention and destruction policy?
    • Are sensitive data stores inventoried and monitored?
    • Are DLP controls implemented for critical data types?
    • Is access to sensitive data logged and reviewed?
    • Are privacy impact assessments conducted for new systems?
    • Is there a breach notification procedure aligned with regulations?
    • Are data transfers to third parties documented and approved?
    • Is data masking or tokenization used for non-production data?
    • Are backups of sensitive data encrypted and tested?
    • Are data access logs retained for audit purposes?

    Security Architecture and Engineering

    • Is there a documented security architecture or target state?
    • Are secure design patterns provided to engineering teams?
    • Are threat models conducted for critical systems?
    • Are security requirements embedded in project intake processes?
    • Is network segmentation used to isolate sensitive environments?
    • Are API security standards defined and enforced?
    • Are security reviews required for high-risk changes?
    • Are infrastructure-as-code guardrails in place?
    • Is a zero trust strategy defined or planned?
    • Are cloud landing zones standardized and monitored?
    • Is application security testing integrated in CI/CD pipelines?
    • Are security exceptions reviewed by architecture governance?

    Vulnerability and Patch Management

    • Are vulnerability scans scheduled for critical systems?
    • Is there a formal vulnerability remediation SLA?
    • Are critical vulnerabilities tracked to closure?
    • Is patching coverage measured for servers and endpoints?
    • Are application security testing results triaged and resolved?
    • Are vulnerability exceptions documented and time-bound?
    • Is risk-based prioritization used to focus remediation efforts?
    • Are external attack surface scans performed?
    • Are dependencies and open-source libraries scanned for issues?
    • Is remediation progress reported to leadership?
    • Are scans integrated into change management workflows?
    • Is vulnerability management integrated with ticketing systems?

    Monitoring and Detection

    • Are critical systems logging to a centralized platform?
    • Is log retention aligned with regulatory requirements?
    • Are detection rules defined for high-risk scenarios?
    • Is there a documented incident triage process?
    • Are alerts tuned to reduce false positives?
    • Are endpoint detection tools deployed across all devices?
    • Are cloud security events monitored and correlated?
    • Is there a process for threat intelligence integration?
    • Are monitoring responsibilities clearly assigned?
    • Are detection gaps identified and tracked?
    • Are monitoring tools tested for coverage and accuracy?
    • Is there a 24/7 monitoring plan or escalation path?

    Incident Response

    • Is there a documented incident response plan?
    • Are incident roles and responsibilities assigned and trained?
    • Are playbooks defined for ransomware and data breach scenarios?
    • Are incident communications templates prepared in advance?
    • Are tabletop exercises conducted at least annually?
    • Is forensic evidence collection process defined?
    • Are legal and regulatory notification steps documented?
    • Is there an incident post-mortem or lessons learned process?
    • Are incident response metrics tracked over time?
    • Are third-party incident response partners on retainer?
    • Are backups tested for recovery in incident scenarios?
    • Is the incident response plan aligned with BCDR?

    Third-Party Risk Management

    • Is there a vendor risk classification model?
    • Are critical vendors assessed prior to onboarding?
    • Are security requirements included in vendor contracts?
    • Is there a process for continuous monitoring of key vendors?
    • Are vendor questionnaires standardized and tracked?
    • Are third-party incidents logged and escalated?
    • Is there an exit strategy for high-risk vendors?
    • Are vendor access rights reviewed periodically?
    • Are vendors required to provide SOC 2 or ISO reports?
    • Are vendors handling sensitive data subject to higher scrutiny?
    • Are vendor risk assessments integrated with procurement workflows?
    • Is there a vendor inventory with ownership and renewal dates?

    Business Continuity and Disaster Recovery

    • Are critical business services identified and prioritized?
    • Are recovery time objectives defined and tested?
    • Are backup processes documented and validated?
    • Are disaster recovery tests conducted regularly?
    • Are backup systems protected against ransomware?
    • Are dependencies on third parties included in continuity plans?
    • Is there a crisis communication plan for major outages?
    • Are alternate work arrangements defined for critical teams?
    • Are business impact analyses updated regularly?
    • Are continuity plans integrated with incident response?
    • Are recovery procedures documented and accessible?
    • Are recovery results reported to leadership?

    Security Awareness and Culture

    • Is security training required for all employees?
    • Are phishing simulations conducted and tracked?
    • Are role-specific training modules provided to engineers?
    • Are security policies communicated during onboarding?
    • Is security awareness included in leadership communications?
    • Are training completion rates tracked and reported?
    • Are rewards or recognition programs used to reinforce behavior?
    • Is there a process to collect employee security feedback?
    • Are contractors and third parties included in training requirements?
    • Is there a clear mechanism for reporting suspicious activity?
    • Are lessons learned from incidents incorporated into training?
    • Is security culture assessed through surveys or interviews?
