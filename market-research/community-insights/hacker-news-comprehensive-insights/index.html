    2. BUG BOUNTY PLATFORMS¶

    Key Discussions¶

    HackerOne

    Bug Bounty Dilemma Take $$ and Sign NDA or Go Public? (July 2025)

    • High-criticality vulnerability in UK company
    • Offered 1,000 bounty with NDA preventing public disclosure
    • Ethical dilemma discussed

    $100M in bounties paid via HackerOne (June 2020)

    • $100M paid by companies using platform (not by HackerOne itself)

    HackerOne appears completely broken (March 2020)

    • Complaints about non-payment
    • Radio silence on Squid RCE vulnerability submission
    • Described as "bordering on outright fraud"

    XBOW autonomous pentester reaches #1 on HackerOne (June 2025)

    • HackerOne described as "economic numbers game"
    • Countless programs with varied difficulty and payouts
    • Primarily offers "supplemental income to infosec-minded school-age kids in the developing world"

    Major Bounty Payouts

    Apple introduces $2M bug bounty

    • Spyware-level exploits eligible

    Sei pays out $2M bug bounty (June 2024)

    • Crypto bug bounties often exceed $1 million
    • Almost all crypto bounties run through Immunefi

    Google paid $10M in bug bounty rewards last year (March 2024)

    • Community argues bounties should be higher
    • Main reason to run program may be optics

    Facebook Historical Payment

    • 2013: Facebook paid researcher $5,000 for vulnerability
    • Enabled payment of education loan in India

    Bugcrowd

    Bugcrowd quality concerns (August 2024)

    • Triage teams "don't comprehend even simple bugs"
    • Dismissed many valid findings
    • Service described as "very expensive"

    Bugcrowd designed to control narrative (January 2025)

    • Platforms keep people from going full disclosure
    • "Lots of gaslighting" in communications
    • Provides control over narrative back to companies

    Income Figures¶

    Top-Tier Payouts:

    • $2,000,000: Apple (spyware-level exploits)
    • $2,000,000: Sei (blockchain)
    • $10,000,000: Google (annual total, 2024)
    • $100,000,000: Total paid through HackerOne platform (cumulative)

    Standard Bounties:

    • 1,000: UK consumer company (high-criticality vuln with NDA)
    • $5,000: Facebook (2013, India)

    Crypto/DeFi:

    • $1,000,000+: Common for crypto bug bounties
    • Most run through Immunefi platform

    Platform Experiences¶

    Positive:

    • Large payouts for critical vulnerabilities
    • Platforms provide legal safe harbor
    • Timestamped submissions protect researchers

    Negative:

    • Frequent duplicate marking issues
    • Payment disputes common
    • NDAs prevent public disclosure
    • Triage quality varies significantly
    • "Economic numbers game" favors volume over quality
    • Companies can side with platform against researchers

    Recommendations¶

    • Research program reputation before investing time
    • Document everything with timestamps
    • Consider going direct to security teams when possible
    • For crypto: Use Immunefi for largest bounties
    • Don't rely on bug bounties as primary income
    • Treat as supplemental income or portfolio building

    Criticisms¶

    • Platforms favor companies over researchers
    • Payout disputes and non-payment issues
    • Duplicate marking used to avoid payment
    • NDAs prevent knowledge sharing
    • Triage quality inconsistent
    • More about optics than actual security
    • Best researchers avoid platforms, work directly

    • Critical insider perspective: "services we sell are disingenuous"
    • Acknowledges helping with practical tasks:
    • Removing CEOs from Domain Admins
    • Implementing MFA
    • Good for learning about large companies
    • Good for learning report writing

    Cryptography/security consultant - what I do (December 2017)

    • "Field that mingles expertise with client satisfaction"
    • Must know something that helps their problems
    • Must convey explanations effectively

    Consulting in cryptography and security design (February 2022)

    • Lean marketing approach: "people send me emails from time to time"
    • No need for full pipeline of gigs when established

    Synacktiv Security Consulting (April 2019)

    • Founded 2012 by consultants with 13+ years experience
    • IT security company doing consulting and auditing

    Income Context¶

    • Consulting rates discussed in separate section
    • Emphasis on expertise + communication skills
    • Work can be intermittent but well-paid when established

    Platform Experiences¶

    • Most consultants work independently or through boutique firms
    • Lean marketing works for established consultants
    • Client satisfaction as important as technical expertise

    Recommendations¶

    Breaking In:

    • Start with current domain expertise (web dev web security)
    • Build specialized knowledge
    • Learn to explain technical concepts to non-technical audiences
    • Document your work (blog, presentations, tools)

    Establishing Practice:

    • Focus on specific niche (cryptography, cloud security, etc.)
    • Build reputation through speaking and writing
    • Word-of-mouth becomes primary lead source
    • Don't need constant pipeline when well-established

    Service Delivery:

    • Mix expertise with client satisfaction
    • Write clear, actionable reports
    • Help companies with practical wins (MFA, privilege reduction)
    • Follow up to ensure implementation

    Criticisms¶

    • Industry accused of being "disingenuous"
    • Some services are "compliance theater"
    • Reports often ignored after payment
    • Varies widely between firms
    • Best consultants rarely advertise

    Key Discussions¶

    Ask HN: What is the highest consulting rate you've come across, in tech? (August 2021)

    • Focus on one-person shops and freelancers
    • Examples of very high consulting rates

    Ask HN: What do you do and what's your consulting rate? (February 2022)

    • IT/digital strategy consulting
    • Connecting incumbent systems
    • Implementing new systems

    $1000+/day consulting rates (May 2018)

    • $3,000 per day for training classes (plus travel and expenses)
    • Consulting opportunities from training and speaking

    Freelancer and consulting rates survey (September 2011)

    • Lower end: $20-$60/hour
    • Higher end: $8,000-$24,000/week

    Major consulting firm rates (February 2011)

    • "When you pay $250/hour for major consulting firm, you get $50 worth of consultant"
    • Heavyweight processes and brand names provide cover for decision-makers

    Tech consultant hourly rates (April 2014)

    • Skilled, experienced developers should not accept less than $75/hour
    • Regardless of location

    Consulting rates vs annual salaries (September 2019)

    • Difficult to get lawyer rates ($200+/hour) with straight hourly
    • Sales and marketing skills + coding = higher earnings

    How much to charge for first freelance consultancy (January 2021)

    • Suggestions ranged from "at least $300/hour"
    • Some suggesting "$500/hour" for specialized expertise

    Income Figures Summary¶

    Hourly Rates:

    • Minimum (experienced): $75/hour
    • Standard consulting: $100-$250/hour
    • Major firms (client pays): $250/hour
    • Specialized expertise: $300-$500/hour
    • Expert networks: $500/hour
    • Training/speaking: $3,000/day + expenses

    Weekly Rates:

    • Standard: $8,000-$24,000/week

    Annual Context:

    • Consulting rates have nothing to do with annual salaries
    • Must account for:
    • Unpaid time between gigs
    • Marketing and sales time
    • No benefits
    • Irregular income
    • Self-employment taxes

    Platform Experiences¶

    • Most consultants work independently
    • Major firms charge 5x what consultant receives
    • Expert networks take cut but provide deal flow
    • Speaking and training generate consulting leads

    Recommendations¶

    Rate Setting:

    • Start with 2-3x your hourly employee equivalent
    • Add premium for specialized expertise
    • Don't undervalue to win business
    • Raise rates regularly as demand increases

    Value Delivery:

    • Focus on highest-paying fields
    • Build sales and marketing skills
    • Create products (training, tools) to complement consulting
    • Speaking engagements generate high-value leads

    Market Positioning:

    • Avoid competing on price
    • Specialize in specific, high-value problems
    • Build reputation through visible work
    • Word-of-mouth becomes primary lead source

    Criticisms¶

    • Major consulting firms take 80% margin
    • Many consultants underprice themselves
    • Difficult to command lawyer rates ($200+/hour) without sales skills
    • Irregular income creates cash flow challenges
    • No one teaches consultants how to price properly

    5. PENETRATION TESTING¶

    Key Discussions¶

    Pwn Santa Container Isolation Bug Bounty/CTF (1 week ago)

    • Challenge to bypass containerization (Dangerzone, gVisor, Podman)
    • Bounties for capturing flags

    Ask HN: Bug Bounty Dilemma (July 2025)

    • 1,000 bounty with NDA for high-criticality vulnerability

    Life as a bug bounty hunter (September 2018)

    • Facebook paid researcher $5,000 in 2013
    • Enabled payment of education loan in India

    Launch HN: MindFort AI agents for continuous pentesting (May 2025)

    • Traditional scanners: 30-50% false positives
    • Manual pentesting: quarterly at best
    • Cost: tens of thousands per assessment
    • Takes weeks to complete

    Show HN: Hackerest Real-time penetration testing (2 weeks ago)

    • Most pentests run for days or weeks
    • Built by security engineers

    Ask HN: How did you get started in Network Security/Penetration Testing? (September 2017)

    • Start with security in your current discipline
    • Web developer: SQL injections, XSS, packet sniffing
    • Get industry certifications (CEH, Offensive Security certs)
    • Most security jobs prefer at least one cert

    Show HN: Oneleet Penetration Testing for SOC 2 (May 2023)

    • Compliance-focused pentesting-as-a-service
    • Schedule and manage tests
    • Vetted testers based in NATO countries

    Show HN: Building a Market for Penetration Testing (February 2016)

    • Retired pen-tester discusses coverage challenge
    • Companies have no idea about coverage
    • Team might have missed issues or found them all

    Penetration testing and low-cost freelancing (October 2020)

    • Common findings: hardcoded admin passwords for debugging
    • Mis-typed HTTP headers like 'set-cookie'

    I work as pentester (freelance nowadays) (September 2023)

    • "Sheer amount of stuff you are supposed to know"
    • Most enter knowing: XSS, PHP basics, SQL injections

    Shared thoughts after 6 years in Pentesting (June 2017)

    • "Don't get certificates. If prospective employer intensely interested, red flag"

    Income Figures¶

    • Tens of thousands: Cost per manual pentest assessment
    • $5,000: Facebook bug bounty (2013)
    • 1,000: UK company high-criticality vulnerability
    • SOC 2 pentests: Required for compliance, pricing not disclosed
    • Freelance rates: See consulting rates section

    Platform Experiences¶

    Manual Pentesting:

    • Takes weeks to complete
    • Costs tens of thousands
    • Happens quarterly at best
    • Coverage difficult to verify

    Bug Bounties:

    • Supplemental income for most
    • Can pay well for critical finds
    • Better than traditional pentesting for some

    Pentesting-as-a-Service:

    • Oneleet: NATO-based testers for compliance
    • Emerging AI-assisted platforms
    • Faster but coverage questions remain

    Recommendations¶

    Breaking In:

    • Start with security in current role
    • Learn web app vulnerabilities first (XSS, SQLi)
    • Practice on OWASP WebGoat, DVWA
    • Consider OSCP certification (not CEH)
    • Build portfolio with CTFs and HackTheBox

    Career Development:

    • Specialize in specific tech stack
    • Document findings in blog/talks
    • Certifications matter for some employers (red flag if too important)
    • Move toward security architecture eventually

    Service Delivery:

    • Set clear scope and coverage expectations
    • Rate coverage, not just vulnerabilities found
    • Follow up to verify fixes
    • Build long-term client relationships

    Criticisms¶

    • Coverage impossible to verify
    • Race to bottom on pricing
    • Many pentesters lack breadth of knowledge
    • Reports often ignored after delivery
    • Certification obsession at some companies
    • AI tools may commoditize basic pentesting

    6. SECURITY AUDITS¶

    Key Discussions¶

    PHP Core Security Audit Results (April 2025)

    • PHP Foundation received ~680K USD last year
    • Funded security audit, sponsored 10 developers
    • Community-funded security initiatives

    Show HN: Security audit that checks what other tools miss (2 weeks ago)

    • 6 checks in seconds
    • A+ to F grades with plain English
    • Fast automated approach

    SSH-audit: SSH server and client security auditing (October 2023)

    • Why don't servers come with secure defaults?
    • Security hardening should be standard
    • Would benefit thousands who aren't security experts

    Squid Caching Proxy Security Audit: 55 vulnerabilities and 35 0days (October 2023)

    • Suggests dropping support for problematic technologies
    • Rather than wasting time fixing them

    TheAuditor Offline security scanner for AI-generated code (September 2025)

    • Verify AI-generated code is production-safe
    • Consistently finds 50-200+ vulnerabilities in AI code
    • Existing tools require cloud uploads
    • Output often too large for AI context windows

    TrueCrypt security audit (June 2014)

    • Cryptography audit different from software security audit
    • Between "public bug bounty" and "summer of code" program

    Mozilla VPN Completes Independent Security Audit by Cure53 (September 2021)

    • Independent third-party audits build trust
    • Cure53 well-regarded in community

    Income Figures¶

    • $680,000: PHP Foundation security budget (annual)
    • Smart contract audits: $20,000+ per audit (mentioned in smart contract section)
    • Third-party audits: Pricing varies by scope and vendor

    Platform Experiences¶

    Traditional Audits:

    • Expensive (tens of thousands)
    • Time-consuming (weeks)
    • Comprehensive when done well
    • Trust requires independent third party

    Automated Tools:

    • Fast (seconds to minutes)
    • Good for known vulnerability classes
    • Miss complex logic issues
    • High false positive rates (30-50%)

    AI-Generated Code Audits:

    • New category emerging
    • Finds 50-200+ vulns consistently
    • AI code requires special tooling
    • Traditional tools struggle with AI output

    Recommendations¶

    Choosing Audit Type:

    • Critical infrastructure: Full manual audit
    • Standard applications: Hybrid (automated + manual review)
    • AI-generated code: Specialized tooling required
    • Open source: Community audit + bug bounty

    Vendor Selection:

    • Check previous work and references
    • Ensure independence from development team
    • Verify security expertise in your tech stack
    • Ask about coverage methodology

    Post-Audit:

    • Prioritize findings by severity and exploitability
    • Fix critical issues before deployment
    • Re-audit after major changes
    • Consider ongoing monitoring

    Criticisms¶

    • Many audits are "checkbox compliance"
    • Expensive doesn't mean comprehensive
    • Reports often too technical for stakeholders
    • One-time audits miss ongoing issues
    • Secure defaults should be standard, not requiring audit
    • Audit industry has perverse incentives (more findings = more work)

    Criticisms¶

    • Many IR "plans" are untested
    • Tools often don't integrate well
    • Communication breakdown common in crisis
    • Expensive commercial tools may not be better
    • AI tools still immature for critical decisions

    Platform Experiences¶

    Traditional Consulting:

    • Expensive ($20K+ just for auditor)
    • Time-consuming (months of preparation)
    • Heavy on documentation and evidence collection
    • Good auditors provide flexibility

    • Control, Vanta, Drata, etc.

    • Continuous monitoring
    • Annual fees + auditor fees

    • Good auditors allow GitHub PRs as change justification

    • Automate evidence collection from day one

    Cost Management:

    • Start with free/open source tools
    • Invest in automation early
    • Budget for internal time (biggest cost)
    • Consider compliance-as-a-service platforms

    Strategic Value:

    • SOC 2 cheaper than endless security questionnaires
    • Required for deals with public companies
    • Good practices should be followed anyway
    • Use as internal process improvement opportunity

    Criticisms¶

    • Called "largely a scam industry" by insider
    • Compliance theater vs. real security
    • Expensive consultants add little value
    • Checkbox exercises that don't improve security
    • Auditors vary widely in quality and helpfulness
    • Small companies disadvantaged by compliance costs

    8. Open source movement growing: Kexa.io, ZeusCloud, MISP, etc. Democratizing security tools.

    9. Real-world pragmatism: Focus on practical wins (MFA, privilege reduction) over perfect security. Mitigations over eliminating all risk.
