## 1. Executive Summary
- Maven is a marketplace-style expert network connecting clients with professionals.
- Experts set their own hourly rates and control availability.
- Clients include consulting firms, investors, corporates, and startups.
- Engagements are typically short, paid calls or surveys.
- Success depends on fast response times, niche positioning, and repeatability.
- Rates vary widely by seniority, domain, and urgency.
- Maven differs from traditional networks by offering more transparency and control.
- This guide focuses on maximizing outcomes while staying compliant.

## 2. Platform Overview
### 2.1 What Maven Is
- Maven is an expert marketplace connecting industry specialists with clients.
- Experts provide insights, market intel, and technical depth on demand.
- The platform supports project scoping, scheduling, and payment workflows.
- It is not a job board; most engagements are short and advisory.
- Maven emphasizes speed, transparency, and self-serve engagement.

### 2.2 Typical Client Segments
- Management consulting firms running strategy and diligence projects.
- Private equity firms evaluating acquisitions and portfolio performance.
- Venture capital investors researching markets, products, or teams.
- Corporate strategy teams benchmarking competitors or new markets.
- Product teams validating features, pricing, or go-to-market plans.
- Legal teams seeking technical context for disputes or expert testimony.

### 2.3 Typical Expert Profiles
- Current or former executives with P&L or strategy ownership.
- Senior managers and directors with hands-on market exposure.
- Product leaders with launch or scale experience.
- Engineers and architects with technical depth in specialized systems.
- Sales leaders who understand buyer journeys and pricing models.
- Operators with supply chain, manufacturing, or regulatory experience.

### 2.4 Geographic Footprint
- Maven supports experts in North America, Europe, and APAC.
- Client demand skews to US and UK time zones but is global.
- Many projects request local market context, so regional expertise matters.
- Global coverage is a differentiator for niche local insights.

### 2.5 Maven in the Expert Network Landscape
- Maven is a tech-forward marketplace with self-serve scheduling.
- Traditional networks like GLG and AlphaSights rely on staffed matching.
- Maven offers more direct control over rates, profile, and availability.
- The marketplace model can mean higher volatility in project volume.

### 2.6 Core Terminology
- Expert: the consultant providing insights through the platform.
- Client: the firm requesting expert consultation.
- Project: a scoped research or diligence engagement.
- Call: a scheduled advisory conversation, often 30–60 minutes.
- Survey: asynchronous questionnaire with fixed compensation.
- Compliance: rules governing what can be discussed and disclosed.

## 3. Business Model and Marketplace Mechanics
### 3.1 How Maven Makes Money
- Maven typically charges clients a platform fee on top of expert rates.
- Experts generally receive their listed rate minus platform processing.
- Some clients pre-negotiate rate caps for entire projects.
- Maven handles contracts, scheduling, and payment processing.

### 3.2 Engagement Flow at a Glance
- Client submits a project brief or searches for experts.
- Maven or the client invites experts to share availability.
- Expert accepts or declines the request based on fit and timing.
- Call is scheduled and confirmed through the platform.
- Expert delivers the call and logs completion if required.
- Payment is processed after client approval or auto-confirmation.

### 3.3 Transparency and Control
- Experts set their own rates and can update them anytime.
- Experts choose which calls to accept and which to decline.
- Experts control availability via calendar and scheduling windows.
- Experts can scope calls by requesting agendas and project context.

### 3.4 Supply and Demand Dynamics
- Some categories are saturated, like generic marketing leadership.
- Others have high demand and low supply, like AI security or biotech.
- Demand spikes around earnings seasons and active deal cycles.
- Response time and profile quality often determine who gets picked.

## 4. Application and Onboarding Process
### 4.1 Eligibility and Prerequisites
- Demonstrated professional experience in a defined domain.
- Ability to speak to recent, real-world decision-making or execution.
- No active conflicts that violate employer or client policies.
- Comfort with compliance boundaries and non-disclosure obligations.

### 4.2 Application Steps
1. Create a Maven account with a professional email.
2. Build your profile with role history, scope, and achievements.
3. Select domains and keywords that match client searches.
4. Set an initial hourly rate based on experience and market context.
5. Submit the application for review.
6. Complete screening or onboarding if requested.
7. Accept expert network agreements and compliance rules.

### 4.3 Screening Interview Topics
- Career highlights and decision ownership.
- Depth of experience in target markets or functions.
- Examples of projects you can discuss without confidentiality risk.
- Comfort level with common diligence frameworks.
- Response speed and availability expectations.

### 4.4 Common Acceptance Criteria
- Clear niche with specific, verifiable expertise.
- Recent and relevant experience in the last 2–5 years.
- Professional communication and concise articulation of impact.
- Credible titles and responsibilities aligned to expertise.

### 4.5 Common Rejection Reasons
- Profile is too broad or generic without clear specialization.
- Experience appears outdated or tangential.
- Insufficient leadership or decision-making authority.
- Unclear impact statements or weak outcomes.

### 4.6 Typical Timeline
- Application review often completes in a few days to two weeks.
- Screening calls may add another week depending on availability.
- Some experts are accepted immediately without a call.

### 4.7 Onboarding Checklist
- Confirm your current employer allows advisory work.
- Read and acknowledge compliance guidelines in full.
- Set availability windows and connect a calendar.
- Prepare a rate card for different types of work.
- Draft a short intro that summarizes your core expertise.

## 5. Profile Optimization and Positioning
### 5.1 Positioning Principles
- Choose a narrow specialty that is easy to understand.
- Anchor your profile around outcomes, not just job titles.
- Use specific metrics to show impact and credibility.
- Align keywords with how clients search for experts.

### 5.2 High-Impact Profile Sections
- Headline: include niche, seniority, and domain keywords.
- Summary: focus on decision ownership and market exposure.
- Experience: describe scope, scale, and measurable results.
- Education and credentials: list only what reinforces expertise.

### 5.3 Example Niche Statements
- "Led pricing strategy for enterprise SaaS at $100M ARR scale."
- "Built supply chain resilience for multi-site medical device plants."
- "Managed regulatory submissions for FDA Class II devices."
- "Scaled growth marketing for fintech targeting SMB lenders."

### 5.4 Keywords That Drive Search
- Include product categories, channels, and geographies.
- Add technical terms or industry standards if relevant.
- Use market labels that investors and consultants recognize.

### 5.5 Profile Update Cadence
- Update every quarter to reflect new responsibilities.
- Refresh keywords after major industry or regulatory shifts.
- Adjust rate as you gain repeat demand or new credentials.

## 6. Hourly Rates and Pricing Benchmarks
### 6.1 How Experts Set Rates
- Maven allows experts to set a public or default hourly rate.
- Many experts start slightly below market to build momentum.
- Rates can be adjusted per request for specialized projects.

### 6.2 Typical Rate Bands by Seniority
- Analyst or specialist roles: $75–$150 per hour.
- Manager or senior individual contributor: $150–$250 per hour.
- Director or VP: $250–$450 per hour.
- C-level or founder: $400–$800+ per hour.
- Highly scarce niches can exceed $1,000 per hour.

### 6.3 Typical Rate Bands by Function
- Product management: $200–$400 per hour.
- Growth and marketing: $150–$350 per hour.
- Finance and FP&A: $150–$300 per hour.
- Engineering leadership: $200–$500 per hour.
- Supply chain and ops: $150–$350 per hour.
- Regulated industries: $250–$600 per hour.

### 6.4 Geographic Rate Considerations
- US experts generally command higher rates than EU or APAC.
- Local market expertise can offset geographic rate discounts.
- Time zone alignment with clients can justify higher pricing.

### 6.5 Urgency and Scarcity Premiums
- Last-minute calls can support a 10–25% rate premium.
- Niche compliance or regulatory experience is highly valued.
- Expertise in fast-moving sectors can justify higher rates.

### 6.6 Rate Setting Worksheet
- Define your target annual consulting income.
- Estimate your available consulting hours per month.
- Divide target income by hours to set a baseline.
- Add a 10–20% buffer for cancellations and admin time.
- Compare with market benchmarks to finalize pricing.

### 6.8 Payment and Invoicing Notes
- Maven handles invoicing and payment collection from clients.
- Experts are paid after completion and client confirmation.
- Payment timing depends on platform policies and client workflows.
- Keep tax documentation ready for year-end reporting.

### 6.9 Rate Adjustment Triggers
- You receive multiple requests per week for the same niche.
- You consistently turn down work due to capacity limits.
- You expand to a higher-responsibility role or industry shift.

## 7. Project Types and Engagement Formats
### 7.1 Classic 1:1 Expert Calls
- 30 to 60 minute calls focused on specific questions.
- Most common format for diligence and market sizing.
- Typically scheduled within 2–7 days of invite.

### 7.2 Multi-Call Projects
- Series of calls with the same client or team.
- Common in deep diligence or operational improvement projects.
- Often require tighter compliance scoping.

### 7.3 Surveys and Async Q&A
- Short questionnaires paying fixed fees.
- Lower time investment and easy to complete between meetings.
- Use surveys to build visibility and early income.

### 7.4 Workshop or Panel Sessions
- Group conversations or panel style discussions.
- Higher prep expectations and often longer sessions.
- May include multiple experts in a moderated format.

### 7.5 Expert Witness or Litigation Support
- Less common and requires strict legal guidance.
- Typically subject to deeper vetting and compliance oversight.
- Compensation can be higher due to specialization and risk.

### 7.6 Recruiting-Adjacent Conversations
- Some clients explore fit for roles via expert calls.
- Keep boundaries clear and do not commit to employment.

### 7.7 Strategy and Go-to-Market Consults
- Market entry, pricing, or channel strategy discussions.
- Emphasis on competitive dynamics and buyer behavior.

### 7.8 Technical Deep Dives
- Architecture reviews, roadmap feedback, or feasibility checks.
- Useful for investors evaluating tech differentiation.

## 8. Success Strategies and Best Practices
### 8.1 Response Speed as a Growth Lever
- Respond to invites within hours whenever possible.
- Decline quickly if the fit is poor to protect ranking.
- Keep availability current to avoid stale slots.

### 8.2 Pre-Call Preparation
- Request the agenda and key questions in advance.
- Confirm compliance boundaries and avoid sensitive topics.
- Prepare a concise narrative of your relevant experience.

### 8.3 Structuring the Call
- Start with a one-minute credibility summary.
- Clarify the client objective and reframe the problem.
- Share insights using a simple framework or taxonomy.
- Offer concrete examples without disclosing confidential data.
- End with key takeaways and next-step suggestions.

### 8.4 Handling Sensitive Topics
- Avoid sharing employer confidential data or non-public metrics.
- Speak at the level of process, drivers, or market patterns.
- Use public sources for hard numbers when possible.

### 8.5 Building Repeat Business
- Offer to clarify any points after the call if permitted.
- Provide a structured summary via the platform if requested.
- Be consistent and reliable to become a go-to expert.

### 8.6 Managing Calendar and Availability
- Use short windows to reduce last-minute conflicts.
- Keep a buffer between calls for notes and resets.
- Avoid overbooking to maintain energy and call quality.

### 8.7 Pricing Strategy Over Time
- Start with a competitive rate to build momentum.
- Raise rates after 5–10 successful calls or repeat invites.
- Adjust upward for high urgency or niche topics.

### 8.8 Communication Quality
- Keep messages short and action-oriented.
- Confirm fit and boundaries in one or two sentences.
- Ask targeted clarifying questions to reduce misalignment.

### 8.9 Continuous Niche Refinement
- Track which keywords drive inbound requests.
- Remove tags that produce low-fit calls.
- Add emerging topics as your industry evolves.

### 8.10 Workload and Burnout Management
- Set a hard monthly cap on consulting hours.
- Decline work that conflicts with your main job priorities.
- Build in a recovery buffer after intense project cycles.

## 9. Compliance, Ethics, and Confidentiality
### 9.1 Core Compliance Principles
- Do not disclose non-public financial or customer information.
- Do not share proprietary product roadmaps or trade secrets.
- Avoid discussing confidential pricing or contract terms.
- Respect all employer policies regarding outside consulting.

### 9.2 Practical Compliance Tips
- Use public information to validate sensitive statements.
- Speak about market trends rather than company specifics.
- If unsure, decline or deflect the question politely.

### 9.3 Conflicts of Interest
- Avoid calls involving direct competitors of your employer.
- Avoid advising on deals that involve your current company.
- Be transparent if any potential conflict exists.

### 9.4 Recording and Documentation
- Do not record calls unless explicitly permitted by the platform.
- Do not share files or data outside approved channels.

### 9.5 Ethical Value Delivery
- Focus on insights, not gossip or confidential anecdotes.
- Respect the spirit of the compliance framework.

## 13. Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them
- Overly broad profiles that dilute your expertise.
- Slow response times that reduce ranking or visibility.
- Accepting calls outside your true expertise.
- Sharing confidential or proprietary information.
- Forgetting to update availability after schedule changes.
- Setting rates too low and attracting low-fit requests.

## 15. Templates, Scripts, and Message Examples

- "My current role focuses on [domain] and I own [decision area]."

### 15.3 Pre-Call Agenda Request

### 15.4 Opening Call Script
- "Quick background: I led [role] at [company] focusing on [scope]."
- "I can cover [three topics] and share lessons learned."

### 15.5 Closing Call Script
- "To summarize, the key drivers are [three points]."
- "If useful, I can share additional context in a follow-up call."

### 15.6 Decline Message Template
- "Thanks for reaching out. This is outside my direct expertise."
- "I want to ensure you find the best-fit expert for this topic."

### 15.7 Compliance Deflection Script
- "I can speak about market patterns, but not non-public company data."
- "I can provide high-level perspectives without confidential specifics."

### 15.8 Post-Call Follow-Up Note
- "Appreciate the conversation. Happy to clarify any points later."
- "Let me know if a follow-up session would be helpful."

## 17. Glossary of Expert Network Terms
- Availability window: time slots visible to clients.
- Compliance training: rules governing expert disclosures.
- Conversion rate: invites that become completed calls.
- Diligence: research performed before an investment or acquisition.
- Expert network: platform connecting specialists with clients.
- Marketplace model: self-serve network with direct matching.
- Match rate: percentage of invites accepted by experts.
- Non-public information: confidential data not publicly available.
- Rate card: documented list of your typical fees.
- Screening: short interview to validate expertise.
- Surge demand: temporary spike in requests for a topic.
- Time-to-first-call: duration from approval to first engagement.
- Utilization: percentage of offered slots that get booked.

## 18. Appendices and Checklists
