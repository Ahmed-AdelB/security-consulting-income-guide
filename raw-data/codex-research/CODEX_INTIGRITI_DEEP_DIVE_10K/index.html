    1. Executive Summary & Platform Overview¶

    1.1 The EU-First Challenger¶

    Intigriti is a European bug bounty platform headquartered in Belgium, known for researcher-friendly triage and strong corporate trust. It has built a reputation as the go-to platform for EU-based programs, especially in regulated industries. Intigriti emphasizes quality over volume, with a smaller but highly curated program catalog versus global giants. For independent researchers, this means fewer duplicates, higher triage quality, and faster feedback loops.

    1.2 Key Platform Signals (Internal Research)¶

    • 50M+ total rewards paid to researchers.
    • 125,000+ registered or verified researchers.
    • Market share signal: 13.9% to 14.7% of platform mindshare.
    • Triage quality rating: 9/10 in internal comparisons.
    • Payment speed rating: 8/10, described as timely.
    • Public sector critical average: 3,348.
    • Manufacturing medium average: 1,150 in 2024 (up from 440).
    • Intel program example: $2,000-$100,000 base with 1.2-1.5x multipliers.

    1.3 Best-Fit Researcher Profiles¶

    • EU-based researchers who benefit from timezone alignment with program owners.
    • Researchers who value high-quality triage over high program volume.
    • Specialists in web app, API, and business logic vulnerabilities.
    • Hunters who prefer deeper program knowledge over broad spray-and-pray scanning.
    • Consultants looking to combine bounties with EU security consulting leads.

    3. EU Market Focus & Regional Advantage¶

    3.1 Why EU Focus Matters¶

    • EU companies are heavily regulated and require structured disclosure.
    • GDPR pressure incentivizes rapid remediation and structured reporting.
    • Many EU programs offer clear scope and compliance-driven response SLAs.
    • EU firms are increasingly adopting bug bounty as a primary security control.

    3.2 Geographic & Timezone Advantages¶

    • EU researchers can coordinate with triage teams during local business hours.
    • Faster back-and-forth reduces report friction and misunderstanding.
    • Multi-language proficiency (EN, FR, DE, NL, ES) helps interpret documentation.
    • Regional familiarity helps spot compliance and privacy edge cases.

    3.3 Sector Concentration in Europe¶

    • Public sector and government agencies appear more frequently in EU programs.
    • Manufacturing and industrial firms are common in EU bug bounty catalogs.
    • EU fintech and payments companies maintain strong access-control scopes.
    • SaaS and enterprise B2B vendors dominate many EU programs.

    3.4 Practical Implications for Researchers¶

    • Focus on GDPR-related data exposure issues that matter to EU firms.
    • Look for multi-region data residency mistakes and cross-tenant access errors.
    • Build tailored test accounts to mirror EU compliance workflows.
    • Expect mature vulnerability triage teams with structured questions.

    4. Business Model & Value Proposition¶

    4.1 Client Value Proposition¶

    • Managed triage and structured disclosure reduce internal security workload.
    • EU compliance alignment improves legal safety for participating organizations.
    • Researcher community provides continuous testing outside scheduled pentests.
    • Platform experience emphasizes quality and researcher collaboration.

    4.2 Researcher Value Proposition¶

    • Smaller crowd than global giants reduces duplicate risk.
    • High triage quality improves report acceptance probability.
    • EU programs often provide clear severity definitions and scope controls.
    • Community events and write-ups reinforce skill growth.

    4.3 Revenue Model Context¶

    • Programs pay platform fees; researchers earn per accepted vulnerability.
    • Intigriti's focus on quality suggests higher acceptance ratios per report.
    • Long-term success depends on report quality and program specialization.

    5. Program Types & Researcher Programs¶

    5.1 Public Bug Bounty Programs¶

    • Open to all registered researchers.
    • Typically smaller scopes but clearer rules of engagement.
    • Best for learning Intigriti workflow and building reputation.

    5.2 Private Invite-Only Programs¶

    • Access granted based on performance, reputation, or manual invites.
    • Lower competition and higher expected value per hour.
    • Often include larger scopes and higher bounty ceilings.

    5.3 Vulnerability Disclosure Programs (VDP)¶

    • No guaranteed payout; focus is coordinated disclosure.
    • Useful for practicing reporting and building a track record.
    • Can unlock private invitations when performance is strong.

    5.4 Program Discovery Tools¶

    • Intigriti Bug Bounty Calculator aggregates 640+ programs across 18 industries.
    • Public program listings show scope, policy, and payout ranges.
    • Researcher dashboards highlight invitations and new program launches.

    5.5 Researcher Enablement Programs¶

    • Intigriti knowledge base and learning resources for platform usage.
    • 1337UP CTF and event series for skill building and visibility.
    • Community write-ups showcase high-impact findings and methodologies.
    • Events and community recognition can improve invite velocity.

    5.6 Researcher Program Strategy¶

    • Use public programs for early wins and practice.
    • Aim for high-quality reports to build trust and invites.
    • Track invite conversions and focus on programs with strong ROI.

    6. Account Setup & Verification¶

    6.1 Account Creation¶

    • Register with a professional handle that aligns with long-term branding.
    • Fill out profile, skills, and relevant security portfolio links.
    • Add prior write-ups, CTF results, or GitHub projects if available.

    6.2 Identity Verification¶

    • Identity verification is required before receiving payouts.
    • Provide valid government ID and accurate personal details.
    • Keep payment details consistent with verification records.

    6.3 Payment Configuration¶

    • Configure supported payout methods as provided by the platform.
    • Ensure your payout profile matches legal and tax requirements.
    • Track payout currency per program; many EU programs pay in EUR.

    6.4 Early Trust Signals¶

    • Submit clear reports with reproducible steps and impact analysis.
    • Avoid low-quality submissions to protect acceptance ratios.
    • Build a reputation for responsive follow-up and collaboration.

    7. Platform Workflow & Mechanics¶

    7.1 Standard Report Lifecycle¶

    1. Recon and scope validation.
    2. Vulnerability discovery and proof-of-concept development.
    3. Report submission with impact, steps, and evidence.
    4. Triage review and validation questions.
    5. Severity scoring and payout decision.
    6. Remediation and coordinated disclosure (if applicable).

    7.2 Reputation & Access Dynamics¶

    • Strong acceptance ratios increase invite velocity.
    • High-severity findings improve researcher credibility.
    • Responsiveness to triage questions builds trust with program managers.
    • Some programs prioritize researchers with consistent high-quality reports.

    7.3 Communication Patterns¶

    • Expect clarifying questions around scope and impact.
    • Provide quick, precise answers to avoid report delays.
    • Keep interactions professional and evidence-driven.

    8. Scope, Safe Harbor, and Rules of Engagement¶

    8.1 Scope Discipline¶

    • Follow scope rules exactly to avoid N/A closures.
    • Validate assets against program scope before testing.
    • Avoid third-party endpoints unless explicitly in scope.

    8.2 Commonly Prohibited Actions¶

    • Denial-of-service or resource exhaustion testing.
    • Social engineering or phishing of employees.
    • Physical security testing.
    • Exfiltration of sensitive data beyond proof-of-access.

    8.3 Safe Harbor Behaviors¶

    • Use test accounts whenever possible.
    • Limit data access to minimal proof required.
    • Document actions to demonstrate safe testing.

    8.4 EU Compliance Awareness¶

    • GDPR breaches are critical issues if data is exposed or mishandled.
    • Sensitive personal data in EU systems is often treated as high severity.
    • Document data residency or cross-tenant access clearly.

    9. Triage, Validation, and Response Quality¶

    9.1 Triage Quality Signals¶

    • Internal triage quality rating: 9/10.
    • Strong replication and technical validation procedures.
    • Researchers report professional and clear communications.

    9.2 Response Time Expectations¶

    • Payment speed rating: 8/10 (timely in internal comparisons).
    • Response times vary by program maturity and scope.
    • Expect faster feedback on private programs with dedicated managers.

    9.3 Tips for Smooth Triage¶

    • Provide step-by-step reproduction with exact parameters.
    • Include impact analysis tied to business risk.
    • Offer a short video or script PoC when possible.
    • Respond quickly to questions to keep the report moving.

    10. Payout Structure & Bonus Mechanics¶

    10.1 Typical Severity Bands (EU Programs)¶

    • Low: 50-300 (informational or limited impact).
    • Medium: 300-1,500 (standard web vulnerabilities).
    • High: 1,500-7,500 (significant access or data exposure).
    • Critical: 5,000-20,000+ (RCE, auth bypass, major data access).

    10.2 Real Data Points from Internal Research¶

    • Public sector critical average: 3,348.
    • Manufacturing medium average: 1,150 in 2024 (up from 440).
    • Intel program base bounty: $2,000-$100,000.
    • Intel multipliers: 1.2x-1.5x on top of base payouts.
    • Intel firmware bug example: $45,000.
    • Intel hardware bug example: $150,000.

    10.3 Bonus and Multiplier Drivers¶

    • Impact scope: deeper impact often triggers higher payout tiers.
    • Quality of report: clear reproduction and business impact can raise rewards.
    • Novelty: unique exploit chains or new attack classes may receive bonuses.
    • Cross-asset impact: vulnerabilities affecting multiple assets can increase payouts.

    10.4 Payout Consistency Tips¶

    • Favor programs with transparent payout matrices.
    • Track per-program payout history if available.
    • Avoid low-paying programs once you have consistent wins.

    11. Payment Methods, Taxes, and Compliance¶

    11.1 Payment Methods¶

    • Payment options depend on program and platform configuration.
    • Many EU programs pay in EUR; some offer USD equivalents.
    • Keep payout details updated to prevent delays.

    11.2 Tax & Documentation¶

    • Maintain records of payout dates, amounts, and program names.
    • EU residents should consult local tax rules for self-employed income.
    • Non-EU researchers should track cross-border reporting requirements.

    11.3 Compliance Best Practices¶

    • Separate personal and bounty earnings in accounting tools.
    • Track income volatility for quarterly tax planning.
    • Create a buffer for delayed payouts and dispute resolution.

    12. Program Selection & Targeting Strategy¶

    12.1 Program Selection Filters¶

    • Favor programs with clear scope and payout matrices.
    • Choose targets aligned with your technical strengths.
    • Prioritize programs with low duplicate risk (private or niche scopes).
    • Avoid programs with outdated assets or unclear policies.

    12.2 EU-Specific Targeting¶

    • Look for EU fintech and SaaS platforms with complex access models.
    • Target B2B enterprise applications with multi-tenant logic.
    • Explore programs tied to EU public sector services for high-impact issues.

    12.3 Portfolio Balancing¶

    • Keep 2-3 core programs for deep focus.
    • Maintain a 5-10 program watchlist for recon and change detection.
    • Rotate public programs for low-hanging fruit only if ROI is strong.

    13. Recon & Asset Discovery Pipeline¶

    13.1 Recon Fundamentals¶

    • Enumerate subdomains and infrastructure assets within scope.
    • Identify new acquisitions and regional domains (ccTLDs).
    • Use passive DNS and certificate transparency to track new assets.

    13.2 EU-Specific Recon Notes¶

    • Many EU firms use localized subdomains (fr, de, nl, es).
    • GDPR-related endpoints often reveal high-impact data flows.
    • Region-specific portals may have weaker access control.

    13.3 Change Detection¶

    • Monitor asset changes daily or weekly.
    • Track new API endpoints and staging environments.
    • Alert on new logins, partner portals, or admin panels.

    13.4 Recon Workflow Example¶

    1. Asset enumeration (subdomains, IP ranges, app IDs).
    2. Live host validation and tech fingerprinting.
    3. Content discovery and endpoint mapping.
    4. Authentication and role mapping for logic testing.
    5. Continuous monitoring for new assets.

    14. Vulnerability Classes with High ROI¶

    14.1 Access Control & Authorization¶

    • IDORs and broken object-level authorization.
    • Role escalation and privilege misconfiguration.
    • Cross-tenant data exposure in multi-tenant apps.

    14.2 Authentication & Session¶

    • Authentication bypass via logic flaws or weak verification.
    • Session fixation and session management issues.
    • MFA bypass or recovery flow abuse.

    14.3 Injection & Execution¶

    • SQL injection or ORM injection in legacy systems.
    • Command injection or deserialization flaws.
    • SSRF leading to internal service access.

    14.4 Business Logic¶

    • Payment or subscription logic bypass.
    • Coupon, discount, or credit abuse.
    • Workflow abuse in onboarding or KYC flows.

    14.5 Data Exposure & Privacy¶

    • Unprotected PII access or misconfigured storage buckets.
    • Insecure direct object references in document systems.
    • Data export features lacking authorization checks.

    15. Report Writing Excellence¶

    15.1 High-Impact Report Structure¶

    • Title with severity and impact.
    • Summary of the vulnerability in one paragraph.
    • Step-by-step reproduction with numbered steps.
    • Proof-of-concept payloads or scripts.
    • Impact analysis tied to business risk.
    • Recommended remediation or mitigation.

    15.2 Report Quality Multipliers¶

    • Include exact request/response pairs.
    • Provide affected endpoints and parameters.
    • Explain attacker prerequisites and permissions required.
    • Use concise language to avoid triage confusion.

    16. Automation & Tooling Stack¶

    16.1 Recon Tooling¶

    • Subdomain enumeration: Amass, Subfinder.
    • HTTP discovery: httpx, naabu.
    • Content discovery: ffuf, feroxbuster.
    • Tech fingerprinting: Wappalyzer, WhatWeb.

    16.2 Vulnerability Testing¶

    • Automated checks: nuclei templates and custom scanners.
    • API testing: Postman, Insomnia, Burp Suite.
    • Web testing: Burp Suite, OWASP ZAP.

    16.3 Workflow Automation¶

    • Daily recon schedules with cron or GitHub Actions.
    • Asset change alerts using diff tools and notifications.
    • Keep a structured notes system for each program.

    17. Private Programs & Invite Strategy¶

    17.1 Invite Acceleration¶

    • Maintain a high acceptance ratio to improve trust signals.
    • Submit fewer, higher-quality reports to protect reputation.
    • Engage with feedback quickly and professionally.

    17.2 Private Program Economics¶

    • Lower competition often yields higher payout per hour.
    • Private programs typically include broader scopes.
    • Expect more structured testing and deeper program context.

    17.3 Private Program Playbook¶

    • Read all program rules and exclusions before testing.
    • Build a dedicated recon pipeline for each private program.
    • Focus on high-impact logic and access control issues.

    18. Community, Events, and Reputation¶

    18.1 Community Channels¶

    • Intigriti community resources emphasize collaboration.
    • Write-ups and case studies highlight successful techniques.

    18.2 1337UP & Competitive Events¶

    • Intigriti hosts 1337UP CTF challenges and community events.

    19. Success Strategies: Beginner to Pro¶

    19.1 Beginner Phase (0-6 Months)¶

    • Start with public programs to learn scope and reporting.
    • Focus on low-hanging issues to build first payouts.
    • Track time spent and build a personal playbook.
    • Join community resources and review published write-ups.

    19.2 Intermediate Phase (6-18 Months)¶

    • Narrow focus to 2-3 programs with strong ROI.
    • Develop specialized knowledge in access control and API testing.
    • Build automation for recon and endpoint discovery.
    • Target medium and high severity issues consistently.

    19.3 Advanced Phase (18+ Months)¶

    • Prioritize private programs and deep product understanding.
    • Chain multiple findings into high-impact exploit narratives.
    • Build a reputation that earns manual invites.
    • Use bounties to feed consulting or training opportunities.

    19.4 Execution Principles¶

    • Depth beats breadth for Intigriti's program style.
    • Quality and clarity beat volume.
    • Track ROI per program and pivot aggressively.

    26. Templates & Checklists¶

    26.1 Report Template (Copy/Paste)¶

    • Title: [Severity] Short impact summary
    • Summary: One-paragraph overview of the issue and affected assets
    • Steps to Reproduce: Numbered list with exact requests and parameters
    • PoC: Script, payload, or video link
    • Impact: Business impact and attacker outcomes
    • Remediation: Suggested fix or mitigation
    • Environment: Browser, OS, account roles, test data

    26.2 Pre-Submission Checklist¶

    • Scope confirmed against program policy
    • Vulnerability reproduced on in-scope asset
    • Impact verified and documented
    • Evidence captured (screenshots, logs, requests)
    • Duplicate check against prior reports or known issues

    26.3 Post-Submission Checklist¶

    • Monitor for triage questions
    • Respond within 24-48 hours
    • Provide additional evidence if requested
    • Document outcome and lessons learned

    26.4 Recon Checklist¶

    • Enumerate subdomains and public assets
    • Identify authentication flows and role boundaries
    • Map sensitive endpoints and file upload paths
    • Test authorization across user roles
    • Review API documentation for hidden parameters

    27. Data Gaps & Verification Notes¶

    28. Appendix: Glossary¶

    • Bounty: Monetary reward for accepted vulnerability.
    • VDP: Vulnerability Disclosure Program without guaranteed payout.
    • Triage: Validation process for submitted reports.
    • Scope: Assets and rules eligible for testing.
    • IDOR: Insecure Direct Object Reference.
    • SSRF: Server-Side Request Forgery.
    • RCE: Remote Code Execution.

    29. Appendix: Severity Mapping¶

    • Low: Informational leaks, low-risk misconfigurations.
    • Medium: Reflected XSS, limited data exposure, CSRF with impact.
    • High: Stored XSS, auth bypass, significant PII exposure.
    • Critical: RCE, full database access, systemic privilege escalation.
